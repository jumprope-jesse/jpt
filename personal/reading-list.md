### Classics & Poetry

- **What in Me Is Dark: The Revolutionary Life of Paradise Lost** by Orlando Reade (Jonathan Cape, 2024)
  - *Added: 2026-01-19*
  - *Source: New Statesman book review*
  - Literary history tracking how Milton's Paradise Lost has been "dug up and dismembered" by successive ideologues
  - Central tension: Milton was both a revolutionary (defended regicide) and devout Christian—giving the epic its fascinating contrariness
  - Traces the poem's use in debates about slavery: Equiano quoting Hell to describe Montserrat plantations; Baron Vastey comparing white colonists to fallen angels; Malcolm X reading Milton in prison and connecting him to Elijah Muhammad
  - The "Mistick Krewe of Comus" in 1850s New Orleans staging Paradise Lost as white supremacist spectacle
  - Reade himself learned to teach the poem to incarcerated students at Princeton—book opens and closes in prison
  - Key insight: The ambiguity between Milton's politics and piety is "fertile ground" that allows contradictory readings
  - Writing style praised: "mercifully unlike most academic works" - witty, sardonic, personal
  - Theme resonates: disobedience, punishment, the creative disruption that births new ideas
  - £22, 272pp

- **The Divine Comedy** translated by Michael Palma (Liveright/Norton, 2024)
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - *Source: Literary Hub excerpt*
  - New translation by award-winning translator (Italo Calvino Award from Columbia)
  - 14,000+ lines of medieval Italian poetry; most translated work in English
  - Despite complexity, readers consistently report being "riveted" - not bored
  - "Ancestor of Lord of the Rings, Harry Potter, Star Wars" - immersive imaginative universe
  - Requires annotated edition (unlike Iliad/Odyssey/Aeneid, cannot be read without notes)
  - See also: knowledge/dante-divine-comedy.md for historical context and reading notes

### Systems Thinking & Problem Solving

- **Thinking in Systems: A Primer** by Donella H. Meadows (Chelsea Green, 2008)
  - *Added: 2026-01-20*
  - *Source: Notion inbox*
  - Classic introduction to systems thinking with half a million+ copies sold
  - Author was lead author of "Limits to Growth" (1972) - first book to model consequences of unchecked growth on finite planet
  - Brings systems thinking from abstract equations into tangible, practical application
  - Core thesis: biggest problems (war, hunger, poverty, environmental degradation) are system failures that can't be solved by fixing one piece in isolation
  - Emphasizes: pay attention to what's important (not just quantifiable), stay humble, stay a learner
  - Practical for problem-solving at scales from personal to global
  - See also: knowledge/complex-systems-failure.md for Richard Cook's 18 principles
  - Relevant: understanding interdependencies in family dynamics and business challenges, leadership approach, navigating complex urban/social systems

### Philosophy & Culture

- **Strange Glory: A Life of Dietrich Bonhoeffer** by Charles Marsh (Knopf, 2014)
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - *Source: The New Criterion review*
  - Comprehensive biography revealing personal flaws alongside heroism
  - Covers his theological evolution: from academic brilliance to Harlem awakening to Nazi resistance to "religionless Christianity"
  - Key themes: costly grace, sin boldly, righteous action beyond the church
  - The ethical paradox of a pacifist blessing assassination attempts
  - See also: knowledge/bonhoeffer-theology-ethics.md

- **American Politics: The Promise of Disharmony** by Samuel Huntington (1981)
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - *Source: NYT Opinion - Carlos Lozada's book for understanding current political moment*
  - Examines what happens when America fails to live up to founding values (liberty, equality, individualism)
  - Identifies cyclical "creedal passion" periods: authority questioned, polarization high, protest high, hostility toward power/wealth
  - Predicted in 1981: "major sustained creedal passion period will occur in the second and third decades of the 21st century"
  - Key insight: "Americans divide most sharply over what brings them together"
  - Famous closing: "America is not a lie. It is a disappointment. But it can only be a disappointment because it is also a hope."
  - Relevant: understanding Trump era, American identity debates, "this is not who we are" rhetoric

- **The Sing Sing Files** by Dan Slepian (2024)
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - *Source: NYT Opinion - Pamela Paul's pick for understanding current moment*
  - Six innocent men at Sing Sing and 20-year fight to free them
  - Author is "Dateline" producer who worked cases on his own time
  - Exposes systemic resistance to admitting wrongful convictions—even in "liberal" NY
  - Broader critique: American vindictiveness, punishment over rehabilitation, disenfranchisement of former convicts
  - Notes: Criminal justice reform has some bipartisan support (First Step Act under Trump)

- **The Sirens' Call** by Chris Hayes
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - Instant #1 NYT Bestseller on attention capitalism
  - Argues attention has become a commodified resource extracted from us (like labor in the 19th century)
  - Offers "big-picture vision" for understanding how tech companies prey on our neurological structures
  - Relevant: parenting in the attention age, work-life balance, reclaiming focus
  - See also: knowledge/attention-economy-critique.md

- **Make Your Own Job: How the Entrepreneurial Work Ethic Exhausted America** by Erik Baker
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - History of how America developed its obsession with entrepreneurial self-reliance
  - Author wrote sharp critique of self-help philosophy genre (see knowledge/self-help-philosophy-critique.md)
  - Relevant to founder life, work-life balance, and NYC hustle culture

### Essays & Long-Form Journalism

- **"In the Rockets' Red Glare"** by Rachel Kushner (Harper's, December 2024)
  - *Added: 2026-01-19*
  - *Source: https://harpers.org/archive/2024/12/in-the-rockets-red-glare-rachel-kushner/*
  - Beautifully written essay about author and her teenage son Remy's immersion in nostalgia drag racing
  - Themes: parent-child bonding through shared mechanical passions, skilled trades vs. passive consumption, "Feingefühl" (fine touch)
  - Sharp cultural critique: "We have been de-skilled, almost invisibly, as if it happened without our consent"
  - On hot-rodding as possession vs. consumption: "To use a commodity is not to possess it. Possession requires mastery."
  - Nostalgia racing as inclusive American subculture—diverse from WWII origins, anti-establishment but patriotic
  - Also covers: nitromethane chemistry, RFC chaplains, community across class/race lines, kids learning trades
  - Relevant: raising kids who make things, Roadkill/HOT ROD Garage YouTube, counterpoint to screen culture

### Military History & Political Science

- **Divided Armies: Inequality & Battlefield Performance in Modern War** by J. Lyall (2020)
  - *Added: 2026-01-19*
  - *Source: ACOUP Fireside Friday - https://acoup.blog/2024/05/10/fireside-friday-may-10-2024/*
  - Central argument: armies with high inequality of membership in the political community suffer battlefield pathologies
  - Introduces "military inequality coefficient" and "battlefield performance index"
  - Case studies from 1800-1942 buttress quantitative analysis
  - Key insight: inequality that matters is "who belongs" not economic or political rights per se
  - Approach: political science methodology with historian's granularity in case studies
  - Technical appendices available online; main text accessible
  - "Well on its way to becoming one of those modern classics of military scholarship"
  - Relevant: understanding organizational cohesion, team dynamics, who feels like they "belong"

### Statistics & Data

- **Statistics** by Freedman, Pisani, and Purves (4th ed.)
  - *Added: 2026-01-18*
  - *Source: cadlag.org book review*
  - The introductory statistics textbook that emphasizes reasoning over technique
  - Key philosophy: statistics augments human judgment rather than supplanting it
  - Strengths: clear writing, Socratic dialogs for counterintuitive topics, excellent real-world examples (polio vaccine trials, unemployment rate estimation)
  - Constantly emphasizes limitations of techniques—"garbage in, garbage out"
  - Antidote to textbooks that produce "someone half-ignorant of statistics" who can fill ANOVA tables but lacks critical thinking
  - Companion: Philip Stark's free online book/lectures at https://www.stat.berkeley.edu/~stark/SticiGui/
  - Relevant: data-driven decision making, understanding research claims, avoiding statistical traps

### Mystery & Crime Fiction

*Added: 2026-01-18*
*Source: NYT "New Mysteries for Summer" - https://www.nytimes.com/2024/05/31/books/review/new-crime-novels.html*

**Series Finales:**
- **The Comfort of Ghosts** by Jacqueline Winspear (Soho Crime, 2024) - 18th and final Maisie Dobbs novel
  - British investigator/psychologist in post-WWII London
  - Investigates orphaned teens squatting in mansion where she once worked as a maid
  - Uncovers decades-old mystery connected to her first husband's death
  - "Wraps up the series with a deft touch"

- **The Last Hope** by Susan Elia MacNeal (Bantam, 2024) - 11th and final Maggie Hope novel
  - WWII spy (former Churchill secretary) assigned to assassinate physicist Werner Heisenberg
  - Set in 1944 Madrid, involves Coco Chanel as antagonist
  - Marriage proposal raises personal stakes alongside impossible mission
  - Doesn't give a happily-ever-after ending—"feels right in a world on the brink"

**Standalone/Other Series:**
- **The Long-Shot Trial** by William Deverell (ECW Press, 2024) - 9th Arthur Beauchamp novel
  - Defense lawyer writing memoirs about 1966 rape/murder case
  - "Shot through with lament and mourning" - author is 87
  - Legal drama with beautifully paced story and final surprises

- **Hall of Mirrors** by John Copenhaver (Pegasus Crime, 2024) - 2nd in trilogy
  - McCarthy-era Washington D.C.
  - Crime-obsessed duo Judy Nightingale and Philippa Watson—now living together as true selves
  - Tracking serial killer from first book
  - "What happens when they meet their favorite crime writer will upend all their lives"
  - Reviewer: "haunted me since I've finished it"

- **The Noh Mask Murder** by Akimitsu Takagi (Pushkin Vertigo reissue, 2024)
  - Japanese crime fiction master
  - Metafictional twist: author is a character, amateur crime writer and friend to narrator
  - Locked-room mystery with cursed 200-year-old Noh mask
  - Family saga of abuse, duplicity, betrayal across generations
  - "Wickedly plotted... plays ruthlessly fair with the reader"

**Why mystery novels?** (from Notion notes)
- Mental escape from work/family demands
- Themes of resilience and problem-solving resonate with engineering/founder life
- Complex moral dilemmas can inform ethical decision-making in tech
- Strong female protagonists exploring identity and societal expectations

### Humor & Comedy Fiction

*Added: 2026-01-19*
*Source: NYT "22 of the Funniest Novels" - https://www.nytimes.com/2024/03/14/books/funny-novels-humor.html*

**Why funny novels?**
- Lighthearted escape amidst work and parenting demands
- Humor as bulwark against conformity and mediocrity
- Wit appeals to the mind; helps maintain balance between seriousness and levity
- "If you know where the funny is, you know everything" (Sheila Heti)

**The List (Post-Catch-22, 1961-Present):**

1. **The Wig** by Charles Wright (1966)
   - Korean War vet in 1960s NYC gets luminous hair relaxer "wig" as gimmick to succeed in white world
   - Electric analysis of racial politics
   - Similar to: Chris Rock's "Good Hair," Eddie Murphy's SNL "White Like Me"

2. **Portnoy's Complaint** by Philip Roth (1969)
   - Lust-ridden young New Jersey man's monologue to his psychoanalyst
   - Mother issues, Jewish assimilation, social class
   - Similar to: "Shiva Baby," "Curb Your Enthusiasm," psychotherapy culture

3. **Oreo** by Fran Ross (1974)
   - Half-Black, half-Jewish woman on quest to find absent father
   - Mixes Yiddish with Black vernacular
   - Resists conventions for novels by Black women—no redemptive uplift, no slavery references
   - Among great American food novels

4. **28 Barbary Lane** by Armistead Maupin (1978-1986)
   - First three "Tales of the City" novels collected
   - San Francisco boarding house with diverse residents
   - Early mainstream work with queer/straight characters on equal footing
   - Elaborate comedy of manners about finding yourself and your community

5. **The Adult Life of Toulouse Lautrec** by Kathy Acker (1975)
   - Dorothy (lonely housewife) falls in love with Larry (giant sea creature)
   - Fish-out-of-water tale tempered by suburban sadness
   - Similar to: Richard Yates novels, "Splash," del Toro's "The Shape of Water"

6. **The Secret Diary of Adrian Mole, Aged 13¾** by Sue Townsend (1982)
   - Adolescent boy in Thatcher-era England with troubled working-class parents
   - Convinced he's an intellectual, submits poems to BBC
   - Naïve observations of complicated adult affairs
   - Similar to: Judy Blume's coming-of-age stories

7. **Heartburn** by Nora Ephron (1983)
   - Roman à clef about marriage to Watergate reporter Carl Bernstein
   - Pregnant cookbook writer Rachel Samstat's witty revenge
   - Rat-a-tat pace of 1940s screwball comedies
   - Includes recipes (sorrel soup, Lillian Hellman's pot roast)
   - Similar to: Shiv/Tom in "Succession," Stanley Tucci's "Taste"

8. **Money** by Martin Amis (1984)
   - John Self, ad man making first feature film (or so he thinks)
   - Debauched transcontinental life, base appetites, offensive thoughts
   - Messy capsule of '80s celebrity and consumption
   - Similar to: "Othello," Dudley Moore in "Arthur," Patrick Melrose novels

9. **The Mezzanine** by Nicholson Baker (1988)
   - Stream of consciousness during one lunch hour
   - Buying shoelaces, eating popcorn, reading Marcus Aurelius
   - Digressive, footnoted, lyrical
   - Pre-smartphone meditation on peculiar thoughts we don't say aloud
   - Similar to: "Seinfeld," Target runs, Samuel Beckett

10. **A Far Cry from Kensington** by Muriel Spark (1988)
    - Sleepless Mrs. Hawkins looks back on life as young war widow and book editor in 1950s London
    - Anonymous threats, fraudulent publisher, radionics pseudoscience
    - Repeatedly calls hack writer "pisseur de copie" (pisser of copy)
    - Similar to: Mysteries, Barbara Pym, unreliable women

11. **American Psycho** by Bret Easton Ellis (1991)
    - Patrick Bateman, Wall Street serial killer from Exeter and Harvard
    - Deadpan satire of blank, soulless '80s generation
    - Skewering of NYC restaurant scene (eagle carpaccio)
    - Similar to: "Bodies Bodies Bodies," "A Clockwork Orange," Huey Lewis and the News

12. **Bridget Jones's Diary** by Helen Fielding (1996)
    - 30-something female Londoner's bullet journal
    - Borrowed from Jane Austen's "Pride and Prejudice" (hero named Darcy)
    - Pre-#MeToo office flirtation, pre-Ozempic weight anxiety, Cool Britannia
    - Inner dialogue raised on women's magazines, crisps, and telly
    - Similar to: "Fleabag," "I Hate Suzie," chocolate

13. **The Poisonwood Bible** by Barbara Kingsolver (1998)
    - Desert Southwest setting, mortality in its messiness
    - Taxidermy, grim nursing home, motherless teenage girls
    - Dead wife's ghost taunts widower
    - Ecological injustice, violent, will make you cry until you laugh
    - Similar to: Noël Coward's "Blithe Spirit," "Blazing Saddles," Sam Shepard

14. **Then We Came to the End** by Joshua Ferris (2007)
    - Ad agency in Chicago during dot-com bust
    - First-person plural narration about workplace collective
    - Impossible pro bono campaign: making people with breast cancer laugh
    - Satire of white-collar culture (Aeron chairs, emails, meetings)
    - Similar to: "The Office," Don DeLillo's "Americana"

15. **The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao** by Junot Díaz (2007)
    - Overweight, nerdy young man desperate to lose virginity
    - History of Dominican Republic under Trujillo
    - "Fukú" curse of the New World
    - Freewheeling Spanglish with footnotes
    - Similar to: "Jojo Rabbit," fast food, Tolkien, "Akira," comic books

16. **I Am Not Sidney Poitier** by Percival Everett (2009)
    - Young orphan named "Not Sidney Poitier" who resembles the actor
    - Tumbles through Poitier's filmography in dream form
    - Lives with Ted Turner ("Can you get fat in a weightless environment?")
    - Racist cops, Klan gatherings, prison chain gang in American South
    - Similar to: Turner Classic Movies, metafiction, Spike Lee films

17. **Lightning Rods** by Helen DeWitt (2011)
    - Entrepreneur creates "lightning rods" for office workers—anonymous sex through wall
    - Productivity up, harassment down
    - Satire pushed to extreme limits
    - Similar to: Entrepreneurship, "Secretary," bathroom architecture

18. **Pym** by Mat Johnson (2011)
    - Black professor leads all-Black expedition to find island from Poe's only novel
    - Find Arthur Pym (200 years old, 200-year-old racism) and giant white beings
    - Satirical fantasy riffing on old adventure tales
    - Similar to: Antiquarian manuscripts, MF Doom, Little Debbie snacks

19. **The Sellout** by Paul Beatty (2015)
    - Young Black man sells artisanal watermelon and weed (strain: "Anglophobia")
    - Father shot dead by police, ends up defending himself at Supreme Court
    - "Your Honor, I plead human"
    - "Bugs Bunny wasn't nothing but Br'er Rabbit with a better agent"
    - Similar to: Craft cannabis, Donald Glover, "Get Out," "American Fiction"

20. **Private Citizens** by Tony Tulathimutte (2016)
    - Millennial friends in pre-2008-crash San Francisco
    - Do-gooder Cory, cynical Linda, porn-addicted Will, passive Henrik
    - Starts as lampoon, gets darker and warmer
    - Similar to: Exhibitionism, David Foster Wallace, "The Big Chill"

21. **My Year of Rest and Relaxation** by Ottessa Moshfegh (2018)
    - Young woman addicted to antidepressants in year before 9/11
    - Ambition to sleep nearly all the time, begins sleepwalking
    - Sleeping through troubled period of history
    - Similar to: "Chaise Longue" by Wet Leg, Trazodone, Fran Lebowitz, Aubrey Plaza

22. **Lake of Urine** by Guillermo Stitch (2021)
    - Sisters Urine and Noranbole Wakeling—men beware
    - Urine sets giant wicker structure spelling suitor's name + obscenity on fire
    - "The time she garroted Timothy Spencer's pony"
    - Characters have USB ports, international conglomerate with exorcist on board
    - Similar to: Mud, Emma Stone in "Poor Things," Monty Python, David Berman

**Why these books matter:**
- Humor is power—"knowing where the funny is" gets closer to truth
- Wit is a sign of trustworthy recognition (Milan Kundera on resisting Stalinism)
- These novels apply satirical tools to race, gender, dating, aging, office life, publishing
