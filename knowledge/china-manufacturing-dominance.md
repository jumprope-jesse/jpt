# China Manufacturing Dominance (2024)

Key data points from Thomas Friedman's Dec 2024 reporting after visiting Beijing/Shanghai.

## Scale
- UN Industrial Development Org projects China will account for **45% of all global manufacturing by 2030**
- This level of dominance seen only twice before: UK at Industrial Revolution start, US after WWII
- Net lending by Chinese banks to domestic industries: $83B (2019) → $670B (2023)

## Electric Vehicles
- BYD Seagull subcompact EV starts under $10,000
- More than half of new car sales in China are EVs
- China building fleet of 170 car-carrier ships (vs 4/year pre-pandemic globally)
- GM took $5B+ write-down on China operations; sales down 59% in 2024

## "Dark Factories"
Fully roboticized factories that operate with lights off:
- 24-hour unattended production
- AI-powered, staffed only by robots
- Engineers visit once daily for maintenance
- Driven by demographic necessity (births fell from 18M to 9M in 7 years)

## Supply Chain Completeness
China has complete domestic ecosystem for manufacturing:
- Any part or rare earth chemical can be sourced domestically
- No other country has this level of supply chain integration
- Enables rapid iteration (some products upgraded every 30 days vs 5-6 years for competitors)

## Demographic Context
- Population projected to decline 100M by 2050
- Possibly 700M decline by end of century
- Robotization essential to maintain productivity with shrinking workforce

Source: NYT Opinion, Dec 2024

## Historical Context: How We Got Here (Ben Thompson, Stratechery 2024)

### Silicon Valley's Original Sin
Within single-digit years of Silicon Valley's founding, chip assembly was exported to Asia:
- 1963: Fairchild Semiconductor opens Hong Kong facility ($0.25/hr vs $2.50/hr US wages)
- 1967: Texas Instruments opens Taiwan facility ($0.19/hr)
- 1969: Fairchild opens Singapore facility ($0.11/hr)

The classic Silicon Valley story of "zero marginal cost" isn't completely honest—the marginal cost was offshored.

### U.S. Government Facilitation
**Tariff Item 807.00** (1963): Articles assembled abroad from US products exempt from tariffs if only value-add is assembly. Average chip assembly value-add: ~$0.01.

**Motivation:** Cold War containment. Foreign policy strategists feared ethnic Chinese communities across Asia "ripe for Communist penetration." Economic integration was a bulwark against communism.

### The Bretton Woods Effect
- 1944: Exchange rates pegged to US dollar
- Made US exports less competitive, but US debt more attractive
- Asian manufacturing profits reinvested in US debt
- US consumer benefited from cheap imports + service economy

Result: Software/design stayed in US (zero marginal cost), manufacturing moved to Asia (experience curve + tacit knowledge accumulation).

### The Modern AI Sandwich
| Layer | Location | Economics |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Chip design (Nvidia) | Silicon Valley | Zero marginal cost |
| Chip manufacturing (TSMC) | Taiwan | Massive tacit knowledge |
| Component manufacturing | China/Asia | Supply chain integration |
| Assembly | Mexico/Vietnam | Cheap labor, easily moved |
| AI deployment/apps | Silicon Valley | Software leverage |

US is "the bread in the AI sandwich"—by design.

### Chinese Software Development
Underappreciated effect of the Great Firewall: created an attractive market for domestic software talent. Xiaomi and Huawei cars run *Chinese* software—Silicon Valley assumed these markets would be theirs forever.

Source: [Stratechery - A Chance to Build](https://stratechery.com/2024/a-chance-to-build/), Nov 2024

## Xi Jinping's Ideological Framework (Dec 2024)

### The Novel: "What Is to Be Done?"
Nikolai Chernyshevsky's 1863 Russian novel—notoriously dense and "unreadable"—has shaped Chinese Communist ideology from Mao to Xi. The novel influenced Lenin, who titled his own pamphlet after it.

Key themes Xi draws from:
- The need for a disciplined vanguard to lead societal transformation
- Rejection of Western liberal individualism
- Long-term strategic patience in ideological struggle
- Self-sacrifice for collective goals

### Relevance to US-China Competition
Xi views competition with America through this ideological lens—not merely economic or military, but civilizational. The struggle is framed as:
- Western liberalism vs. Chinese socialism with Chinese characteristics
- Short-term democratic volatility vs. long-term authoritarian planning
- Individual rights vs. collective national rejuvenation

This ideological grounding explains China's willingness to accept short-term economic pain (tariffs, decoupling) for long-term strategic positioning.

Source: [NYT Opinion - This Unreadable Russian Novel Drives Xi's Struggle Against America](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/12/15/opinion/china-brics-xi-jinping-trump.html), Dec 2024
