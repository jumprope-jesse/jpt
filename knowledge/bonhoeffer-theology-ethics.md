# Dietrich Bonhoeffer: Theology, Ethics, and Resistance

The legacy of anti-Nazi theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906-1945)—theologian, double agent, and martyr.

---

## Source

- [The Ambiguous Witness of Dietrich Bonhoeffer](https://newcriterion.com/article/the-ambiguous-witness-of-dietrich-bonhoeffer/) - The New Criterion (2014), review of Charles Marsh's biography "Strange Glory"

## The Man

Bonhoeffer came from a wealthy, close-knit family of "enlightened skeptics and humanists" where religion played no serious part. His decision at 13 to become a theologian surprised everyone. His intellectual brilliance was extraordinary:

- First doctorate in theology *summa cum laude* at University of Berlin (1927), age 21
- Karl Barth called his dissertation "Sanctorum Communio" a "theological miracle"
- Completed his Habilitationsschrift at 24—"Act and Being"—considered "one of the great theological achievements of the twentieth century"

**Personal flaws**: Arrogant, condescending, lifelong dandy obsessed with wardrobe, emotionally immature, relied on parents financially his whole life. "Emotional resources unequal to his intellectual ones."

## Theological Journey

### Early Position: Christ Exists as Community

Central claim of "Sanctorum Communio": "Christ exists as community"—focused not on abstract theology but on the sociology of the church, the lived life of faith.

Combined German Protestant liberalism (Harnack's view that the simple gospel had been obscured by institutional church) with Barth's neo-orthodox critique (liberals had led Christianity into reductionist naturalism).

### The Union Seminary Transformation (1930-31)

Bonhoeffer found Union Seminary intellectually vacant: "There is no theology here." Students "completely clueless" about basic Christian dogmatics. Even Niebuhr seemed to talk about "everything but God."

But two experiences transformed him:

1. **Abyssinian Baptist Church in Harlem**: Adam Clayton Powell Sr.'s "joyous, full-throated expression of Christian worship"—traditional doctrines of sin and grace combined with concern for the poor. Bonhoeffer carried recordings of Negro spirituals for life.

2. **Social Gospel exposure**: Niebuhr and others opened up activist Christianity—tenement ministries, trade-union work, civil liberties. Bonhoeffer came to respect those for whom "the gospel required a response of genuine, self-giving concern for those at the margins."

**Key insight**: His year transformed his theology "from the phraseological to the real." Grace became "a divine verdict requiring obedience and action."

### The Anti-Hitler Years

From January 30, 1933 (Hitler named chancellor), Bonhoeffer defined his life against the Nazi regime. Two days later, he was on Berlin radio denouncing the *führer* principle.

His critique was theological, not merely political: **Hitler was the Antichrist, Nazi ideology heresy, and opposition a theological imperative.**

The German Evangelical Church largely caved. The *Deutsche Christen* (German Christians) pursued a *Volkskirche* based on common blood, with Germans as "the new Israel." They endorsed the Aryan paragraph removing Jews from civil service, including churches.

**Bonhoeffer's radical position**: "A church that excluded Jews was a church without Jesus Christ and thus no church at all." Most fellow dissenters found this too extreme.

### Finkenwalde and "Costly Grace"

From 1935-1937, Bonhoeffer ran a seminary for the Confessing Church, envisioning "a new monasticism" combining orthodox doctrine, practical training, and communal life of "uncompromising discipleship."

His lectures became **"The Cost of Discipleship"**:

> "A polemic against the Lutheran tendency to portray faith as a refuge from obedience."

Central concept: **Costly grace**—"When Christ calls disciples, he bids them come and die." Liberation of the gospel is inseparable from the burden of the cross.

### The Resistance and Ethical Paradox

As the Confessing Church became irrelevant, Bonhoeffer turned to secular resistance. He became a double agent with the Abwehr (military intelligence), passing classified material to the Allies while ostensibly working as a Nazi spy.

**The ethical paradox**: Bonhoeffer's pacifist creed contradicted his blessing of assassination attempts on Hitler (over 100 of them). He adopted Niebuhr's Christian realism:

> In profound moral dislocation, there is no escaping complicity in evil. Violent resistance or tacit acceptance of monstrous cruelty—guilt either way.

He chose to **"sin boldly"** (Luther's phrase)—sin for the sake of righteousness.

His "Ethics" (unfinished) wrestled with the fact that his resistance colleagues included more humanists and atheists than Christians. He developed the category of **"natural piety" and "unconscious Christianity"**—"the righteous" extended beyond the church to all who contended for justice, truth, and human decency.

## Prison Theology: "Religionless Christianity"

Arrested April 1943, Bonhoeffer spent 18 months in Tegel prison. His "Letters and Papers from Prison" contain his most controversial ideas:

### Core Claims

- A "world come of age" requires "religionless Christianity"
- The witness of faith must be limited to "prayer and righteous action"
- Intellectual integrity requires discarding "the working hypothesis of God"—living "as if there were no God"
- "Saving souls" is irrelevant to the biblical message
- "What matters is not the beyond but this world, how it is created and preserved, is given laws, reconciled, and renewed"
- To have faith is simply, like Jesus, "to be there for others, especially the distressed and the excluded"

### The Problem

> "What real work is 'God' or 'Jesus' doing here? Why clutter what seems essential unbelief with empty anachronisms?"

The austere neo-agnosticism of the Letters coexisted with traditional Scripture, prayers, hymns in his personal practice. **He lived in dual theological worlds with incompatible assumptions.**

### The End

Executed April 9, 1945, at Flossenburg concentration camp on direct orders from Hitler. Evidence suggests he died not by gallows but **dangling by rope on a flexible hook, toes barely touching ground, in prolonged agony of suffocation.**

His last messages returned to orthodoxy:

- To Maria von Wedemeyer: gratitude for "God's mercy and forgiveness" and assurance that his sins were covered "by the forgiving love of Christ crucified"
- To Eberhard Bethge: he died "in the certainty of forgiveness and in the hope of eternal life"

## Key Tensions and Takeaways

### 1. The Paradox of Costly Grace
Authentic faith requires action and sacrifice, not merely intellectual assent. "Faith as a refuge from obedience" is a corruption.

### 2. Sin Boldly When Necessary
When all options involve complicity in evil, choose the lesser evil actively rather than the greater evil through inaction. Accept the guilt.

### 3. The Righteous Beyond the Church
People of conscience who fight for justice, truth, and decency may be "unconscious Christians"—agents of grace regardless of their beliefs.

### 4. This-Worldliness
Focus on "how the world is created and preserved, given laws, reconciled, and renewed"—not on escape to the beyond.

### 5. The Incomplete Synthesis
Bonhoeffer never resolved the tension between his radical "religionless Christianity" and traditional faith. His prison theology may represent thought *in extremis*—"hard cases make bad law."
