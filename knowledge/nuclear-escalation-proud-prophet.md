## Proud Prophet (1983)

A secret Pentagon war game that ran for two weeks at Fort McNair, unique in nuclear gaming history because it:
- Was largely unscripted and allowed to play to natural conclusion
- Involved highest levels of military (Secretary Weinberger, Gen. Vessey participated secretly)
- Used actual classified war plans, communication channels, and doctrine
- Ran continuously through global command channels

### Key Participants
- **Paul Bracken** - Yale professor, served as chronicler with full access
- **Thomas Schelling** - Harvard economist, game theory pioneer, designed the game
- **Phillip Karber** - Former Marine, set up the exercise for Weinberger
- **Caspar Weinberger** - Secretary of Defense, played secretly behind a stand-in

---

## The Game's Progression

### Days 1-4: Conventional Phase
- Soviet forces maneuvering in Eastern Europe
- Biological agents released against Bonn (confirmed by Day 4)
- NATO defensive positions along 440-mile front
- Soviet chemical weapons hit Ramstein and NATO air bases

### Day 5: First Nuclear Use
- As Belgian/Dutch forces neared collapse near Hamburg
- US authorized first limited "tactical" nuclear weapons
- Only 11 nuclear artillery rounds fired (subkiloton yields)
- Goal: control escalation

### Days 5-7: Escalation Spiral
- Red team responded with own low-yield nuclear artillery
- Both sides fired over their own forward positions
- Exchanges thickened the line, turned "Blitzkrieg into Sitzkrieg"
- Then longer-range systems engaged airfields
- Theater weapons (3x Hiroshima) hit airports, harbors, depots

### Final Phase: Global Devastation
- Paris, London, Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Brussels destroyed
- Every major German and Polish city gone
- Strikes on Sweden, Belarus, Baltics, Japan, South Korea, Philippines
- Final hotline message from Weinberger to Moscow: "May you burn in hell like you are going to burn here"

## Key Insights

### Communication Failure
- Hotline was in daily use but distrust prevailed
- Each side thought simultaneously it was winning (or losing)
- Battlefield became opaque - "operating at best at 50% knowledge"
- Neither side found the other's explanations of "limited" strikes reassuring

### The Core Lesson
**Nuclear escalation cannot be controlled.**

The reaction was "shock that it had gone all the way." This shaped US strategy for decades - talk of controlling nuclear escalation fell from favor.

### Paul Bracken's Observations
1. "For generating situations you don't anticipate, gaming is the best method... somebody else is playing the enemy, and you're not having to think up what the enemy might do"
2. Both sides tried to minimize civilian casualties but accepted large-scale annihilation would follow
3. The distinction between "counterforce" (military) and "countervalue" (cities) broke down rapidly

## Current Relevance

### The New Arms Race
- Global arsenal dropped from 70,000 warheads (Cold War peak) to ~12,000 today
- Now growing again per Federation of American Scientists
- US, Russia, China all investing heavily in modernization
- Key treaties expired or abandoned
- China pursuing arsenal to rival US and Russia

### Destabilizing Factors
1. **Smaller, more precise weapons** - tempting to use on battlefield
2. **Cyberattacks + advanced surveillance** - concerns about first-strike capability
3. **Multipolar nuclear world** - North Korea, Pakistan, Iran add complexity
4. **Communication erosion** - less attention to de-escalation art

### Extended Deterrence Problem
De Gaulle's question to Kennedy: "Is America really willing to trade New York for Paris?"

The fundamental issue: convincing adversaries you'll do something "incredibly irrational and risk committing national suicide" for someone else's homeland.

## Strategic Critique

### Bracken on Pentagon Modernization
"Building a harder command-and-control system using blockchain so we can get the 'go' code to the missile forces is an improvement on one of the most fantastically unlikely scenarios... I'm looking at real-world threats and dangerous pathways to nuclear war. I don't think a bolt from the blue is one of those."

The Pentagon applies "high levels of skills to the wrong problem."

### The Real Risk
Not surprise attack, but **escalation in plain sight**:
- Conventional conflict goes wrong
- Nuclear saber rattling
- First use of small battlefield weapons
- Counteruse
- Uncontrollable slide toward extinction

## Russia's 2024 Nuclear Doctrine Revision

Source: David E. Sanger, NYT (Nov 19, 2024)
https://www.nytimes.com/2024/11/19/us/politics/russia-nuclear-weapons-ukraine.html

### Key Changes to Russian Doctrine
Putin formally announced a revised nuclear doctrine on the 1,000th day of the Ukraine war:

1. **Expanded use criteria** - Now permits nuclear weapons not just in response to existential threats, but for any "critical threat" to sovereignty and territorial integrity
2. **Targeting non-nuclear states** - First time Russia declared right to use nuclear weapons against states with only conventional arms, if backed by a nuclear power (clearly aimed at Ukraine backed by US/UK/France)
3. **Concurrent timing** - Announced same day Biden authorized Ukraine to use ATACMS missiles deep into Russian territory

### US Response: Calculated Dismissal
- Biden administration called it a "signaling exercise"
- No change to US nuclear alert levels
- National Security Council statement condemned but showed no alarm
- Matthew Bunn (Harvard): "The actual short-term probability of Russian nuclear use hasn't increased"

### Expert Assessment
Vipin Narang (MIT, recently Pentagon): "Even with these revisions to Russian doctrine, I'm still very confident that U.S. and NATO conventional and nuclear posture are capable of deterring Russian nuclear employment, and restoring deterrence should Putin miscalculate."

Key insight: **"A nuclear threshold is not determined by words, but by the deterrence balance and stakes."**

### The Normalization Problem
The Ukraine war has "inured Washington and the world to the renewed use of nuclear weapons as the ultimate bargaining chip." What once would convene the UN Security Council now evokes shrugs.

Historical context:
- October 2022: US intelligence detected conversations among Russian generals prompting fears of tactical nuclear use
- Biden told NY fundraiser they were closer to nuclear exchange than since Cuban Missile Crisis
- It didn't materialize, reinforcing a pattern of threat â†’ dismissal

### Strategic Landscape Shift

**Post-Cold War optimism is dead:**
- Obama 2009: Envisioned world without nuclear weapons
- 2024: Putin placed nuclear weapons in Belarus
- 2026: New START treaty expires with no replacement in sight
- Bipartisan talk of expanding US arsenal for Russia-China partnership

**The uncomfortable truth:** Putin's message is "not that nuclear weapons are back, but that they never went away."

### Connection to Proud Prophet Lessons
This real-world scenario validates Bracken's assessment of the "real risk" - not a bolt from the blue, but escalation in plain sight:
- Conventional conflict (Ukraine) going wrong for Russia
- Nuclear saber rattling (doctrine changes)
- Potential first use of small battlefield weapons
- The slide continues...

The difference from 1983: We're watching it unfold incrementally rather than in a two-week war game.

## The Presidential Nuclear "Football"

Source: National Security Archive, George Washington University (July 18, 2023)
https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/briefing-book/nuclear-vault/2023-07-18/presidential-nuclear-football-eisenhower-george-w-bush

### What Is the Football?

Since the late 1950s, U.S. military personnel traveling with the President carry a special case known variously as the "satchel," "black bag," "emergency actions pouch," or most commonly, the "Football." It enables the President to make decisions about nuclear weapons use in the event of a sudden military crisis.

**Contents include:**
- Emergency action plans for nuclear response
- Presidential Emergency Action Documents (PEADs) - proclamations and executive orders for national emergencies
- Cryptographic orders to the Joint Chiefs authorizing nuclear retaliation
- Authentication codes

### Presidential Emergency Action Documents (PEADs)

These classified documents, originating in the Eisenhower administration, derive presumed legal authority from "extraordinary powers of the President under the Constitution."

**Known or suspected contents (per declassified records):**
- Declaration of unlimited national emergency and civil defense emergency
- Suspension of habeas corpus
- Declaration of martial law
- Authorization of mass arrests and arbitrary detentions
- Office of Censorship for communications crossing U.S. borders
- Authority to assume functions of state/local governments
- Power to take over state governments deemed "unable or unwilling to perform essential civil functions"
- National resources mobilization orders

**Legal concerns raised by officials:**
- Edward McDermott (Office of Emergency Planning, 1960s): Some documents of "doubtful legality"
- William Odom (Brzezinski's military assistant): Troubled over "legality" of some PEADs
- FEMA Director John Macy (1980): Inappropriate to seek DOJ clearance for documents relying on "implied" presidential powers

### Historical Evolution

**Eisenhower era:** Emergency Action Documents (EADs) established
**Kennedy era:** Military aide always near President; Football included "emergency war orders"
**Johnson era:** First public reporting (1965); LBJ reportedly found the arrangement stressful; declined a Football as VP
**Carter era:** Extensive review; Hugh Carter Jr. found PEADs "obsolete given the total devastation which could be expected from a thermonuclear attack"
- VP Mondale assigned his own Football (first VP confirmed to have one)
- Updated PEADs completed by May 1980

### Operational Details

- Military aide travels with President at all times
- When President is in Washington, satchel stays at White House
- Authorization can be sent by telephone, teletype, or microwave radio
- Messages routed through Defense Communications Agency to NORAD or Strategic Air Command

### The Fundamental Question

Federal Emergency Plan D-Minus (1959) outlined proclamations including one declaring a national emergency. But as the declassified documents note:

> "Left unsaid was an imponderable: Who, after nuclear war had begun, would be around to implement, much less obey, such edicts?"

This connects to Proud Prophet's core lesson: the gap between theoretical control mechanisms and the reality of nuclear escalation.
