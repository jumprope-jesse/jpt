# El Salvador's Transformation Under Bukele

Source: Matt Lakeman's "Notes on El Salvador" (March 2024)
https://mattlakeman.org/2024/03/30/notes-on-el-salvador/

## The Problem: World's Highest Murder Rate

El Salvador's homicide rate peaked at 139 per 100,000 in 1995 (highest in modern history). For comparison:
- Baltimore during The Wire era: ~40
- US peak (1980): 10.2
- Canada/Lithuania today: ~2.4
- France/Germany: 0.5-1.1

The rate remained 40-107 from 2002-2018, consistently among the world's highest.

## Origin of the Gangs

### Civil War Displacement (1980-1992)
- 1+ million Salvadorans fled the civil war
- ~500,000 went to Los Angeles, mostly as illegal immigrants
- Settled in impoverished Hispanic neighborhoods, faced harassment from Mexican gangs

### MS-13 and B-18 Formation
- **Barrio 18 (B-18)**: Under Mexican Mafia, uniquely accepted any Hispanic members
- **Mara Salvatrucha (MS-13)**: Formed by Salvadoran teenagers in LA, heavy metal culture, machetes as trademark weapons
- Both developed "criminal capital" through US prison system
- War between them began late 1980s, continues 30+ years

### The Deportation Catalyst
- 1996 immigration law increased criminal deportations
- 1998-2014: 300,000 deported to Central America
- Deportees brought gang organization, criminal expertise to El Salvador
- By 2016: 60,000-70,000 gang members, ~500,000 collaborators (8% of population)
- Gangs operated in 94% of municipalities, extorted 70% of businesses
- Cost: ~$4 billion annually (15% of GDP)

## Key Insight: Why Salvadoran Gangs Are So Dangerous

**MS-13's annual revenue in 2016: $31.2 million** - divided among 40,000 members = $65/month/person (half minimum wage).

The paradox: Their weakness makes them more dangerous.

Drug cartels (Colombia, Mexico) are commerce-oriented - violence is a cost to minimize. Salvadoran gangs are honor-oriented:
- Little money at stake in gang wars
- Violence doesn't hurt their business model (extortion)
- May even make extortion more lucrative through fear
- Leaders consistently end up in jail (unlike cartel bosses in mansions)
- Members get extensive tattoos despite making them targets

This explains 20+ years of bloodshed with minimal strategic purpose.

## Failed Approaches

### Mano Dura (2003) - Conservative Crackdown
- Arrested 20,000 suspected gang members rapidly
- Initial success: crime dropped
- Problem: 85-95% released within 6 months when Supreme Court struck down laws
- Legislature withdrew support (FMLN opposition)

### Super Mano Dura (2006)
- Slower, more measured approach
- Modest results, didn't prevent FMLN election victory

### Gang Truce (2012-2013) - Liberal Approach
- FMLN regime negotiated with gang leaders
- Concessions: Better prison conditions, reduced raids, $20M employment subsidies
- Results: Murder rate dropped from 71 to 41-42
- Problems:
  - Stats may have been juked (gangs hiding bodies instead of leaving them)
  - Government gave gangs cell phones, improving their organizational capacity
  - Massive corruption scandal when full terms revealed
  - Truce collapsed by mid-2013
  - Murder rate spiked to 107 in 2015

## Bukele's Rise

### Background
- Born 1981, Palestinian-Arab Christian family
- Father: Islamic imam who established 4 mosques, opened El Salvador's first McDonalds, polygamist with 6 wives
- Dropped out of law school, managed nightclub, ran PR firm for FMLN

### Political Trajectory
- 2011: Mayor of Nuevo Cuscatlán (pop. 8,000) - age 30
- 2015: Mayor of San Salvador (metro 2M)
- 2019: President at age 37
- Ran on anti-corruption, anti-establishment platform
- Used social media masterfully, bypassed traditional media
- Created "Nuevas Ideas" party (cyan branding, personal loyalty)

### Personality Assessment
"An immature and impulsive man, addicted to his phone and social media. He couldn't go 30 minutes without checking Twitter... always very scattered. It's hard for him to focus and get organized."

But also: non-corrupt (rare for El Salvador - 4 of 5 post-civil-war presidents were convicted of corruption), highly intuitive, excellent campaigner.

## The Territorial Control Plan (TCP)

### Seven Phases
1. **Preparation**: 6,500 troops deployed to 12 municipalities, prison lockdowns
2. **Opportunity**: Healthcare, sports centers, schools ($158M)
3. **Modernization**: Police/military equipment upgrades ($109M)
4. **Incursion**: Expand into prime gang territory
5. **Extraction**: Large military operations targeting gang holdouts
6-7. (Unnamed)

### Results Through 2021
Murder rate fell from 38 (2019) to 18 (2021) - all-time low, but unclear if TCP or continuation of existing trend.

## The 2022 Crackdown

### Trigger
March 26-27, 2022: 87 murders in 72 hours (highest post-civil-war rate). Possibly gangs flexing after secret truce collapse.

### Response
- "State of exception" declared
- Carte blanche to police/military
- Habeas corpus suspended
- Arrests based on "suspicious appearance," "nervousness," "having tattoos"

### Scale
- 77,000 arrested in months
- Prison population: 40,000 → 105,000 (1.7% of population)
- El Salvador now most imprisoned country on earth
- Built world's largest prison (Terrorism Confinement Center): 40,000 capacity

### Results
- Murder rate: 18 (2021) → 2.4 (2023)
- Now comparable to Canada
- ~2/3 of gang members arrested
- El Faro (critical outlet) concluded: "the gangs do not exist in this moment as El Salvador knew them for decades"

## Why 2022 Succeeded Where Others Failed

1. **Intensity**: More troops, faster deployment, looser rules
2. **Speed**: Gangs couldn't organize response or flee
3. **Timing**: B-18 had split, MS-13 nearly splitting - both organizationally weak
4. **Political support**: Bukele controlled all three branches, 75%+ approval
5. **TCP groundwork**: Years of embedding forces in neighborhoods provided intelligence
6. **Alliance for Prosperity**: US aid improved law enforcement capability
7. **Luck**: Right place, right time, right conditions

## The Democratic Critique

Bukele's undemocratic actions:
- Marched soldiers into legislature (Feb 2020)
- Replaced Supreme Court with loyalists (May 2021)
- Gerrymandered districts, reduced seats
- Undermined critical media
- Ignored court rulings
- Ran for immediate re-election despite constitutional prohibition
- Extended "state of exception" indefinitely (legally 30 days)

Counter-argument:
- Democracy wasn't serving Salvadorans when gangs controlled 94% of municipalities
- Citizens care more about safety than voting for "corrupt political parties in a corrupt democratic system"
- Bukele was elected democratically and maintains 85%+ approval
- 2024 re-election was free and fair

## Framework: When Authoritarianism Might Be Justified

The article argues there's a theoretical tipping point where democratic institutions become so dysfunctional that undemocratic reset is preferable. Conditions that applied to El Salvador:
- 25 years as murder capital of the world
- Tens of thousands killed
- Democratic solutions repeatedly failed (both left and right approaches)
- Corruption endemic across all parties
- Citizens terrorized, economy crippled

Historical precedents cited: Jerry Rawlings (Ghana), Ataturk (Turkey), possibly Napoleon.

Key risk: Concentrating power is high-risk/high-reward. Good dictator can accomplish more, but bad dictator can do more damage.

## Bukele's Bitcoin Experiment

### What Happened
- Sept 2021: Bitcoin made legal tender (world first)
- $30 BTC given to every citizen via Chivo wallet
- Government bought 2,845 BTC (~$121M)

### Results
- Most citizens immediately spent their BTC, never acquired more
- 86% of businesses never conducted a Bitcoin transaction
- Only 1.9% of remittances in BTC
- Bitcoin City (planned volcano-powered mining city) appears abandoned

### Financial Outcome
- Initially underwater as BTC fell from cycle peak
- Entered profit Dec 2023 when BTC passed ~$42K
- Currently showing significant gains

## Key Quotes

On the crackdown:

His Twitter bio evolution: "The Coolest Dictator in the World" → "Philosopher King"

## Takeaways

1. **Gangs aren't always profit-driven**: Honor-oriented gangs may be more dangerous than wealthy drug cartels precisely because they have less to lose
2. **Deportation policy backfires**: US criminal deportations created the gang problem by injecting criminal capital into a weak state
3. **Democratic dysfunction can justify extraordinary measures**: When institutions fail for decades, citizen welfare may trump procedural norms
4. **Timing and luck matter**: Same basic strategy (mass arrests) that failed in 2003 succeeded in 2022 due to changed conditions
5. **Non-corruption buys leeway**: Bukele's apparent honesty (rare for Salvadoran presidents) gives him credibility despite other flaws
