# Remote Work Productivity Research

## Overview
Research examining whether working from home increases or decreases productivity, with nuanced findings about trade-offs between mentorship and short-term output.

Source: [Axios - Does working from home boost productivity?](https://www.axios.com/2024/01/23/remote-work-productivity-wfh-report) (Jan 2024)

## Key Research Findings

### Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco Study
- **Scope**: 43 industries in the private sector
- **Finding**: No meaningful effect on productivity during the pandemic period
- **Implication**: At the macro level, remote work didn't significantly change productivity rates

### New York Fed / UVA / Harvard Study
More nuanced findings from one Fortune 500 company (software engineers):

#### Pre-Pandemic Baseline
- **Close proximity teams**: All in one building
- **Dispersed teams**: Spread across buildings (mimics WFH)

#### Mentorship vs. Productivity Trade-off

**Close Proximity Benefits:**
- More code feedback from senior to junior engineers
- Better mentorship opportunities
- BUT: Lower short-term productivity, especially for senior coders (busy mentoring)

**When Remote Work Started:**
- Mentorship gap between arrangements disappeared
- Junior workers who received early in-person mentorship were more likely to get raises
- Close-together workers had higher quit rates (better training = better opportunities elsewhere)

#### Business Adaptation
- Companies shifted from "building talent" (mentorship) to "buying talent" (hiring better-trained workers)
- Suggests businesses adapt their strategies based on work arrangements
- May explain why macro productivity didn't change significantly

## Measurement Challenges

**Labor Department Approach:**
- Productivity = Output รท Hours Worked
- Simple for manufacturing (widgets produced)

**Knowledge Work Complexity:**
- Outputs are harder to measure
- WFH is most prevalent in knowledge work
- Makes productivity assessment much trickier

## Implications

1. **Short-term vs. Long-term**: Proximity boosts mentorship (long-term gain) but may reduce immediate output
2. **Talent Development**: Remote work may shift companies toward hiring experienced workers rather than developing juniors
3. **Individual vs. Organizational**: What's good for individual productivity may differ from what's good for organizational learning
4. **Economic Adaptation**: Businesses adjust strategies to match work arrangements, potentially neutralizing productivity impacts

## Related Considerations

- Junior engineer career development in remote-first environments
- Knowledge transfer mechanisms without physical proximity
- Company hiring strategies in hybrid/remote contexts
- Long-term effects on organizational learning and innovation
