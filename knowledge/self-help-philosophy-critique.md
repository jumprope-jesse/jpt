# Self-Help Philosophy Critique

Critical analysis of the modern self-help philosophy genre, particularly pop-Stoicism and the tension between acceptance and political action. Also includes more thoughtful alternative frameworks for thinking about happiness and fulfillment—including practical techniques that avoid the genre's pitfalls.

---

## "How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love My Shitty Life" (Erik Baker, The Drift)

*Source: https://www.thedriftmag.com/how-i-learned-to-stop-worrying-and-love-my-shitty-life/ - Added: 2026-01-18*

A historian's critique of the self-help philosophy genre—books that promise to help you "make peace with the reality that disappointment, frustration, and agony are inexorable parts of life."

### The Genre's Core Message

Self-help philosophy brands itself as an "antidote" to toxic positivity and unrealistic expectations. Key figures:

- **Mark Manson** - *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a Fuck*, *Everything Is Fucked*
- **Ryan Holiday** - Pop-Stoicism empire (Daily Stoic, *The Obstacle Is the Way*, etc.)
- **Oliver Burkeman** - *Meditations for Mortals*, "The Imperfectionist" newsletter

Their shared advice: Accept limits on your control, moderate expectations, question assumptions about what matters. "We make ourselves miserable by worrying about stuff we can't affect."

### The Critique

**It's not as subversive as it claims:**
> "If all this advice really cuts against the grain of the modern world... the fact that, in various incarnations, it has proven so popular (and so lucrative) for so long is a bit of a puzzle."

It's less an "antidote" to positive thinking and more its "Janus face"—looking back serenely at hopes society raised and crushed.

**Pop-Stoicism's platitudes:**
> "Of things some are in our power, and others are not."

Baker notes this is "so platitudinous that its primary effect is neither to enlighten nor to corrupt, but simply to bore."

**The political risk:**
Quoting Marcuse: "It would suit those in power to know that those they have made anxious... are content to wallow in their anxiety and not take any action to reform the material conditions that brought it about."

### Historical Context

The genre has roots in:
- **New Thought** (Gilded Age) - material obstacles are illusory, overcome through proper focus
- **Midcentury occultism** - astrology, flying saucers, Jungian collective unconscious
- **1960s-70s counterculture** - Zen, existentialism, homesteading, psychedelics
- **Alan Watts, Robert Pirsig** - blending Eastern philosophy with lifestyle advice

Stoicism spiked in the 1990s—not an era of complacent optimism but one where "forces determining social reality feel even more impersonal than before."

### The Better Books

Baker identifies authors who take the political critique seriously:

**Kieran Setiya** (*Life Is Hard*) - Uses Frankfurt School ideas but argues "there is value in a single step toward justice."

**Avram Alpert** (*The Good-Enough Life*) - Unlearning "greatness" expectations can prevent burned-out organizers. Manson's advice is "pretty good" but must include appreciation for structural forces.

**Samir Chopra** (*Anxiety*) - Directly presents Marcuse's critique. "Socially manufactured anxiety... can and should be redressed. Combating and confronting anxiety requires acceptance, activism, and contemplation."

### The Central Tension

> "The question, then, is what the proper blend looks like."

Baker's personal realization:
> "I was spending more of my time thinking about my own despair than about the problems outside myself that were supposedly fueling it."

The trap of over-acceptance:
> "With enough practice tolerating imperfection you can learn to forget what it is you're failing to live up to. When those expectations include owning a vacation home or winning a Nobel Prize, letting go might be healthy—but doing so is tragic when they include stopping a genocide or ending homelessness."

### Key Insight: Collective vs. Individual Agency

> "Any reality made by human beings can be remade by them. The price of this power is mutual obligation: we can never let ourselves off the hook."

The positive-thinking cliches are true **in the first-person plural**:
- **We** are responsible for how our lives unfold
- **We** can do things that seem impossible
- But this requires "acting through structures that are bigger than any of us alone"

> "What we do with our lives does matter—as much as anything possibly could. That should keep us up at night."

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Don't use acceptance as an excuse** - Distinguish between existential limits and socially manufactured constraints
2. **Collective action expands your sphere of control** - Things that seem beyond individual control aren't beyond collective control
3. **Watch for the self-focus trap** - If you're spending more time processing your despair than addressing its causes, recalibrate
4. **Both optimism and resignation can be self-indulgent** - The point is engagement, not comfort

### About the Author

Erik Baker is a historian at Harvard and author of *Make Your Own Job: How the Entrepreneurial Work Ethic Exhausted America*.

## "The Blessing and Curse of Ambition" (David Brooks, NYT)

*Source: https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/24/opinion/ambition-self-improvement.html - Added: 2026-01-18*

A framework for channeling ambition productively without being consumed by it. Brooks argues you don't have to renounce ambition entirely—instead, wrestle with it through five constituent struggles.

### The Central Problem

Ambitious people are energetic and achieve big things, but risk becoming "ruthless, manipulative, status-obsessed and so focused on worldly success that they become hollow inside."

The question: **How do you live a driven life without becoming a jerk?**

### Mixed Motives Are Okay

Brooks rejects the "die to self" approach for most people:

> "If I'm going to get through the arduous work of, say, writing a book, I need to put my egotistic desires at the service of my loftier desires. I start the book hoping it will be helpful to people, but to propel me to work on it for years, I also need my name on the cover."

Lincoln as patron saint: "his ambition was a little engine that knew no rest" yet he spent his life wrestling with it, "like Jacob wrestling with the angel."

### The Five Struggles

**1. Craft vs. Reward**
- Intrinsic desire to be good at what you do vs. extrinsic rewards (money, fame)
- Key ratio: if intrinsic motivation isn't dominant (~70-30), you're on "morally perilous ground"
- The Sammy Glick archetype: doesn't care about the craft, only the rewards

**2. Gift Love vs. Need Love** (from C.S. Lewis)
- **Gift love**: emerges from fullness, pours outward, delighted with the world
- **Need love**: emerges from a void, voracious, insatiable, laced with fear of failure
- Need love bends a person inward and leads to isolation
- "People whose ambition is fueled by resentment (Nixon and Trump) are fueled by need love"

**3. Excellence vs. Superiority**
- **Noncomparative striving**: being good is its own reward
- **Competitive striving**: need to be better than others, zero-sum thinking
- Meritocracy is built on superiority striving (rankings, exclusive schools)
- "Frustrated striving for superiority often seeks relief in the form of aggressive self-deception in which the superior is cast as morally deficient"

**4. High vs. Low Desires**
- Some desires are morally superior: friendship > popularity, community > Porsche
- The trap: wanting love but thinking popularity/status will get you there
- "Idolatry is an ultimate longing for a finite thing"
- "Be careful what you love... you end up turning into what you love" (Augustine)

**5. Ambition vs. Aspiration**
- **Ambition**: desire to rise higher in the world (social mobility)
- **Aspiration**: desire to become a better person (inner transformation)
- Aspiration demands "renouncing the merit badge life"
- Walter Kirn quote: "Instead of filling in the blanks, I wanted to be a blank and be filled in"

### Key Insight

> "Professional success often comes from being wholehearted, from moving unreservedly after one goal. But the people we admire are often divided against themselves, burning hot with some ambition while trying to transcend the flames."

The internal struggle itself is healthy—not something to resolve but to maintain.

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Check your craft-to-reward ratio** - Is your intrinsic motivation dominant?
2. **Examine the source** - Does your drive come from abundance or hollowness?
3. **Watch for superiority thinking** - Is your satisfaction comparative or intrinsic?
4. **Audit your desires** - Are you chasing what you actually want or its proxies?
5. **Cultivate aspiration alongside ambition** - Inner growth matters more than external markers

## "Being Too Ambitious Is a Clever Form of Self-Sabotage" (Maalvika, Substack)

*Source: https://maalvika.substack.com/p/being-too-ambitious-is-a-clever-form - Added: 2026-01-18*

A deep dive into how perfectionism disguised as ambition prevents creative work. The author, who writes about tech and creativity, explores why the gap between imagination and execution paralyzes us—and how to overcome it.

### The Core Problem: Murder of the Perfect

> "Creation is not birth; it is murder. The murder of the impossible in service of the possible."

Before creation begins, work exists in perfect form in your imagination—flawless because it's nothing. The moment you begin making something real, you kill that perfect version. We learn to love the imaginary version too much.

### The Taste-Skill Gap (Ira Glass's "The Gap")

Your taste (ability to recognize quality) develops faster than your skill (ability to produce it). This creates torment:

- Watch a child draw—fearless, unselfconscious, no curse of sophisticated taste
- Around age 8-9, taste arrives like a harsh critic
- Most people stop drawing not from lack of talent but from ability to judge before ability to execute
- "We become connoisseurs of our own inadequacy"

### Productive Avoidance

The brain's elegant escape from the taste-skill gap:

> "Staying busy with planning, researching, and dreaming while avoiding the vulnerable act of creating something concrete that might fail."

Examples:
- Wannabe founders listening to podcasts on loop
- Wannabe TikTokkers watching hours of videos as "research"
- Wannabe novelists spending years on character backstories for books they never begin

It feels like work because it engages intellect. It functions as avoidance because it protects from imperfection.

### The Photography Class Experiment

Jerry Uelsmann at University of Florida:

- **Quantity group**: 100 photos for A, 90 for B, etc.
- **Quality group**: One perfect photo

**Result: All the best photos came from the quantity group.**

The quantity group learned what can't be taught:
- Excellence emerges from intimacy with imperfection
- Mastery is built through befriending failure
- A hundred conversations with light, a hundred experiments in composition

The quality group spent the semester in "theoretical purgatory"—experts in the map while the quantity group explored the territory.

### Your Brain Is an Exquisite Liar

Neuroscience of goal substitution:

> "When you imagine achieving something, the same neural reward circuits fire as when you actually achieve it."

- Planning *feels* satisfying because neurologically it *is* satisfying
- Athlete visualizing routine = optimizing existing skills
- Aspiring novelist crafting outlines = getting reward without doing the work
- "The brain can't tell the difference between productive preparation and elaborate procrastination"

### The Quitting Point

The moment initial excitement fades and work reveals its true nature:

- Writers: around page 30 when inspiration runs out
- Entrepreneurs: after months when market doesn't respond
- Artists: when they see their work objectively

This separates quantity from quality mindsets:
- Quantity group is intimate with imperfection—each attempt is data, not judgment
- Quality group interprets early struggles as evidence something is wrong

> "Most creative projects die... not from lack of talent or resources, but from misunderstanding the nature of the work itself."

### The "Do-Learn" Philosophy

From Olin College of Engineering:

> "Do-Learn. Those two words contain a revolution."

Not "learn-then-do" (must earn permission to act). The radical idea that **doing is learning**—understanding emerges from hands as much as head.

Applications:
- Learning cooking: burn onions, discover how heat actually behaves
- Learning language: stumble through conversations with native speakers
- Learning YouTube: post videos, let brutal feedback teach what resonates

### Reality as Collaborator

> "Reality has opinions about your work that are often more interesting than your own."

When you lower stakes and begin:
- Reality shows what works and what doesn't
- Reality introduces happy accidents
- Reality is the collaborator you didn't know you needed

Standards emerge through process, not proclamation.

### The Social Media Comparison Trap

Algorithmic machinery has erased the process that makes mastery visible:

- Time-lapse of masterpiece: millions of views
- Real-time video of hundredth mediocre attempt: algorithmic obscurity
- Instagram shows finished painting, never failed color experiments
- We begin to believe learning should be immediately visible

> "We see the oak tree, never the acorns. The symphony, never the scales."

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Lower the stakes** - Permission to create your worst is the path to creating your best
2. **Trust the quantity approach** - Make many imperfect things to find excellence
3. **Recognize productive avoidance** - Planning that feels like work but produces nothing
4. **Push through the quitting point** - Early struggles are where real work begins
5. **Let reality teach you** - Engage with materials, not just theories
6. **Reclaim beginner's privilege** - Permission to be bad at something

### Key Quote

> "Your masterpiece won't emerge from your mind fully formed like Athena from Zeus's head. It will emerge from your willingness to start badly and improve steadily."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to Brooks's ambition framework**: Both address the pathology of excessive ambition, but from different angles—Brooks focuses on moral character, this piece on creative paralysis
- **Relates to Erik Baker's critique**: Both challenge the idea that internal work alone solves problems—action in reality is necessary
- **Relates to programming philosophy**: The "rough drafts" approach, "simplest thing that could possibly work," and joy of experimentation all echo these themes
- **Relates to learning frameworks**: The "do-learn" philosophy aligns with deliberate practice through isolated difficulty

## "Flounder Mode" - Kevin Kelly's Alternative to Tortured Ambition (Colossus)

*Source: https://joincolossus.com/article/flounder-mode/ - Added: 2026-01-18*

A portrait of Kevin Kelly (co-founder of WIRED, futurist, author) as a role model for ambitious work without suffering. The author—a former Stripe employee who spent years chasing "cool" projects over career ladders—visits Kelly to make peace with her own professional choices.

### Kevin Kelly's Resume

Kelly isn't known for one "big thing" but a constellation of influential work:
- Editor for the Whole Earth Catalog (1980s)
- Co-founded WELL, one of the first online communities (1985)
- Co-founded WIRED magazine (1993)
- Spielberg's "futurist advisor" on Minority Report
- Inspiration for the "Death Clock" on Futurama
- The "1,000 true fans" concept that launched the creator economy
- Early writer on quantified self → Fitbit, Strava, Apple Watch, Oura Ring
- Currently helping build a 10,000-year clock in West Texas with Brian Eno and Jeff Bezos

Naval Ravikant called him a "modern-day Socrates." Marc Andreessen said "everything Kevin Kelly writes is worth reading."

### The "Hollywood Style" of Working

Kelly works "Hollywood style"—a series of creative projects rather than building a unicorn. His guiding principle: **"Following my interests."**

When asked how this differs from shiny object syndrome:
> "The people who become legendary in their interests never feel they have arrived."

On money as motivation:
> "I think one of the least interesting reasons to be interested in something is money."

He cites Walt Disney: "We don't make movies to make money. We make money to make more movies."

On greatness:
> "Greatness is overrated. It's a form of extremism, and it comes with extreme vices that I have no interest in. Steve Jobs was a jerk. Bob Dylan is a jerk."

### The Tortured Ambition Counter-Examples

The author contrasts Kelly's joy with tech's dominant narratives:
- Reid Hoffman: "If a founder brags about having 'a balanced life,' I assume they're not serious about winning"
- Jensen Huang: wants to "torture people into greatness"
- A serial founder who started his second company "out of chaos and revenge"
- A CEO who looks in the mirror and asks "Why do you suck so much?"
- Elon Musk: "seems tortured"
- Sam Altman: rumored to have gotten scurvy from only eating ramen while building his first startup

Facebook's Little Red Book: "Greatness and comfort rarely coexist."

### The Author's Career Anxiety

The author describes her own "lily-padding" career at Google, gaming startups, and Stripe—always choosing interesting projects over management tracks:
- Rejected a manager's suggestion to build a five-year career plan
- Scoffed at people who cared about titles
- Became "the person you ask to have coffee with when you wanted to quit your job and do something weird"

Then anxiety crept in. She watched peers who chased titles and promotions seemingly having more impact:
> "They were mentoring young talent, influencing top lines and bottom lines, and had their fingerprints on all kinds of cool industry-recognized work."

She felt "unqualified" and "full of regret about not picking a lane."

### Kelly's Reframe

On illegible career paths:
> "Kevin Kelly would say it's good to have an 'illegible' career path—it means you're onto interesting stuff."

On the bottomless well:
> "The more you pursue interests, the more you realize that the well is bottomless."

### The Core Insight

Kelly's approach to work is notably:
- Joyful and buoyant, not angsty
- No suffering or ego
- Not about finding market holes or global domination
- Measured by internal satisfaction, not net worth
- Work unfolds slowly through genuine interest, not sheer force of will
- Impact through teaching and advancing collective thinking, not winning

> "I want more role models like Kevin Kelly. People that proudly whistle while they work. Who have boundless energy and healthy gums. Whose enthusiasm is contagious. Who are well-adjusted and emotionally regulated. Who have solid relationships and happy families. Who are hungry and impactful and care deeply, without being jerks."

### The Permission Being Sought

The author realizes:
> "I wasn't there with Kelly for permission to work Hollywood style. I was there for permission to work with both ambition and joy."

### Key Takeaways

1. **Following interests isn't scatterbrained** - The distinction is continuous movement and the feeling of never having "arrived"
2. **Greatness comes with extreme vices** - You can opt out of that tradeoff
3. **Illegible career paths signal interesting work** - Not a failure to pick a lane
4. **Impact without suffering is possible** - Joy and ambition can coexist
5. **The well is bottomless** - Genuine interests generate endless energy and projects

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to Brooks's ambition framework**: Both acknowledge mixed motives are okay, but Kelly goes further—suggests you can sidestep the tortured-genius trope entirely
- **Complements the "Too Ambitious" piece**: Both challenge the necessity of suffering for creative work
- **Extends Baker's critique**: If acceptance/Stoicism is one overcorrection to hustle culture, Kelly offers another path—joyful engagement without the guilt
- **Tension with conventional career advice**: The author's anxiety came from comparing herself to "ladder climbers"—Kelly suggests that's the wrong comparison

## "Clearly Incorrect" - Quarter Mile

*Source: https://quarter--mile.com/Clearly-Incorrect - Added: 2026-01-18*

An essay on how experts and authority figures spread misinformation without prioritizing truth, using Gordon Ramsay's debunked cooking advice as the central example.

### The Problem: Bullshit Without Malice

The author argues that Ramsay's widely-repeated steak advice—letting meat rest before cooking—is scientifically incorrect. Chef J. Kenji Lopez-Alt debunked the claims about even cooking and searing, yet the myth persists through blind trust in authority.

This illustrates "bullshit" (in the Harry Frankfurt sense): communication that lacks concern for truth. The speaker isn't lying—they simply don't care whether what they're saying is accurate.

### Examples Across Fields

The pattern appears everywhere:
- **Doctors** prescribing unnecessary antibiotics
- **Physical therapists** recommending ineffective ice treatments
- **Teachers** pushing debunked "learning styles" theory
- **Startups** blindly copying "best practices"

### The Solution: Personal Accountability

Rather than assuming malice, the author advocates individual responsibility:
1. **Prioritize accuracy** in your own communications
2. **Model honest behavior** for others
3. **Verify before repeating** - resist the urge to pass along unverified claims

The positive example: a doctor who consults 200 peers before giving advice, demonstrating commitment to getting it right.

### Key Insight

> "Building a more trustworthy society requires individual effort to verify information, resist blind trust, and personally commit to accuracy over convenience."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Complements the Baker/self-help critique**: Both challenge blind acceptance of authority—whether self-help gurus or celebrity chefs
- **Relates to scientific skepticism**: The importance of empirical testing over appeal to authority
- **Practical epistemics**: Focus on what you can control—your own commitment to truth

## "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit" (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/aGnRcBk4rYuZqENug/it-s-okay-to-feel-bad-for-a-bit - Added: 2026-01-18*

A critique of excessive Stoicism and Buddhist detachment, arguing for a middle path where negative emotions are allowed and useful—but not cultivated.

### The Danger of Over-Detachment

The author shares a pivotal experience:
> "About a decade ago I meditated for an hour a day every day for a few weeks, then sat down to breakfast with my delightful (at the time) toddlers and realized that I felt nothing. There was only the perfect crystalline clarity and spaciousness of total emotional detachment."

This crystalline clarity wasn't enlightenment—it was hollowness. The author stopped meditating and never went back.

### The 60/90 Rule

On emotional work:
> "For most people—once you have fixed about 60% of your emotional reactivity and 90% of your psychological triggers, you have hit a point of diminishing returns."

Past that point, further investment in "nonreactivity" and "unattachment" becomes pathological from the perspective of actually being happy and doing things with your life.

### The Right Response to Setbacks

When things don't go your way:
> "The problem isn't that you felt bad about it. The problem isn't that you are insufficiently stoic. The problem isn't that you care too much about the outcome. The problem... is that things didn't go your way."

The solution isn't meditation on impermanence or visualizing your mother's funeral. It's:
1. Determine what you can do to practically address the actual situation
2. Act upon your decision
3. Accept that you'll feel somewhat bad during this process—and that's okay

### Negative Emotions as Motivation

Emotions serve functional purposes:
- **Guilt** motivates avoiding choices you'll regret
- **Anger** (in a healthy mind) helps overcome fear
- **Fear** motivates risk avoidance and calibrates your risk sense through experience

Suppressing these creates a bias toward inaction. Engaging with them creates integration and self-trust.

### The Action Orientation

> "The more you expose yourself to the risk of feeling bad for a bit, the more experience you gain, and the less likely you are to make a serious mistake that would cause you to feel very bad for a long time."

Emotional detachment leads to analysis paralysis. Action, even when uncomfortable, leads to growth.

### The Bumper Sticker Problem

> "But it's hard to fit 'Take Epictetus 60% Seriously, Meditate for 20 Minutes per Day for 3 Months and Then Stop' on a bumper sticker."

Nuanced advice doesn't go viral. The extremes—total Stoic detachment or unchecked emotionality—are easier to communicate.

### Key Insight

> "It's okay to care about things. I know that somehow this doesn't sound deeply wise but I think sometimes we are too wise for our own good."

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Don't over-optimize for detachment** - The goal isn't to feel nothing
2. **Use negative emotions functionally** - Let them motivate action, not just suffering
3. **Address the external problem** - The issue is usually the situation, not your reaction
4. **Accept diminishing returns** - After fixing major reactivity, stop self-optimizing
5. **Bias toward action** - Processing emotions through action beats processing through rumination

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Direct counter to Epictetus's advice**: "If you kiss your child... you will not be disturbed if either of them dies" - the author explicitly rejects this as going too far
- **Complements Baker's critique**: Both argue that excessive internal focus can become self-defeating
- **Echoes Brooks on internal struggle**: Both value maintaining productive tension rather than resolving it entirely
- **Relates to the "quantity" approach**: Exposure to risk of feeling bad builds experience and calibration

## "The Cult of Doing Business" - Review of *Make Your Own Job* (Erik Baker)

*Source: https://www.commonwealmagazine.org/calvert-work-entrepreneur-ethic-baker-review-job - Added: 2026-01-18*

Drew Calvert's review of Erik Baker's *Make Your Own Job: How the Entrepreneurial Work Ethic Exhausted America* provides deep historical context for why we're pressured to treat work as a calling.

### The Core Problem

One of the strangest features of American work culture: the constant pressure to treat one's job as something more than a job—a calling, a means of expressing oneself, a vehicle for personal growth.

Baker calls this self-help ideology "the rot festering at the core" of our national obsession with work.

### Historical Origins

**New Thought (Gilded Age)**: A nineteenth-century spiritual movement responding to capitalism's trauma:
- Writers like Ralph Waldo Trine (Henry Ford's favorite) believed "creative" people could manifest opportunities
- William James addressed it in *The Varieties of Religious Experience*
- Claims: "Personal thoughts are forces," "serenity" and "happiness" are available through right attitude
- Function: "Promised to restore power to the individual" amid labor market precarity
- Eventually evolved into "doctrinally fuzzy success philosophy"

**The Con Artists**:
- **Napoleon Hill** (*Think and Grow Rich*, 1937): Con man who invented meetings with Andrew Carnegie, committed credit fraud, oil-stock scams, charity embezzlement
- **Dale Carnegie**: Emphasized turning talents and passions into cash
- Both preyed upon millions recovering from the market crash

**Norman Vincent Peale** (*The Power of Positive Thinking*):
- "The more you lose yourself in something bigger than yourself, the more energy you will have"
- That "something bigger" notably didn't include unions or New Deal infrastructure
- Eventually officiated Donald Trump's first wedding
- Book never out of print

### The Psychology-Business Marriage

The entrepreneurial ethic now uses psychology as its primary idiom:
- Business schools employ teams of psychologists
- Concepts: "grit," "flow," "growth mindset," "personal brand"
- "It's not always obvious where positive psychology ends and entrepreneurship begins"

**Key figures**:
- **Abraham Maslow** (coined "positive psychology" in 1954): Saw "entrepreneurial leadership" as best application of his theories
- **Martin Seligman**: Emphasized positive psychology's usefulness for success at work. *Flourish* was blurbed by Tony Hsieh
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi**: Author of *Flow* became author of *Good Business: Leadership, Flow, and the Making of Meaning*

The incentive to make insights amenable to executives and consultants is irresistible.

### The Tragedy of Tony Hsieh

The book opens with Zappos CEO Tony Hsieh, author of *Delivering Happiness: A Path to Profits, Passion, and Purpose*. He died in a fire at 46 after barricading himself in a shed with nitrous-oxide canisters, propane, and candles. A cautionary tale about positive psychology's Faustian bargain with startups.

### The German Social Science Connection

**Max Weber**: Studied Arbeitsfreude ("joy in work"). His 1904 US visit convinced him of the Protestant ethic thesis—that worker motivation came from treating work as calling.

**Joseph Schumpeter**: Celebrated the entrepreneur as heroic force behind "creative destruction"—the leader who inspires joy, meaning, purpose.

**The dark undertones**: "The entrepreneur became a Nietzschean conqueror, setting the world ablaze with his energy and virility." Baker describes Steve Jobs as "the most ruthless entrepreneur to emerge from the counterculture."

### Why It's Exploitative

Celebrating workers' "proactivity" disguises an exploitative relationship:
- "Autoletic" workers (intrinsically motivated) are more easily exploited
- Those who treat jobs as extension of themselves can be burdened with preserving their own well-being
- Since Americans "prize psychological health as highly as a religious duty," failing to be entrepreneurial becomes a moral failing

During the consulting boom of the 70s-80s, "value creation" and "innovation" served as alibis for cost-cutting and deregulation.

### The Tragic Cycle

> "On the one hand there is burnout, a hustle culture whose intensity many Americans feel is grinding them into mental, emotional, and physical depletion. On the other hand, there is the lingering poison of deindustrialization, the anomie or social decay that appears to creep into communities where work disappears."

These are two sides of the same coin:
- Specter of worklessness motivates punishing self-demands when working
- Relentless optimization renders swaths of workforce redundant
- On and on, in a perpetual spiral

### The Function of the Ethic

> "The ultimate function of the entrepreneurial ethic is to reconcile workers to precarity."

It creates the illusion that tech billionaires and gig workers (no benefits, no union) are aligned against government regulation.

Because the ideology is custom-made for slumps and busts, it can never be discredited: "Bad news for the economy has always been good news for the entrepreneurial work ethic."

### The "Brand Called You" Problem

Tom Peters in Fast Company (1997): "We are the CEOs of our own companies: Me, Inc."

This pressure to build a personal brand is pathological because:
1. The LinkedIn startup crowd is insufferable
2. A personal brand is currently looting the federal government with help of a lunatic entrepreneur
3. Most importantly: it corrupts souls and relationships

### The Reviewer's Conclusion

> "There is nothing wrong with finding spiritual value in one's career—provided that value doesn't exist to be exploited by a billionaire. And of course it's fine to love what you do. But it's even better to treat love itself as the most important work."

### Key Takeaways

1. **The entrepreneurial ethic has deep historical roots** - New Thought → positive thinking → positive psychology → startup culture
2. **Psychology became the language of exploitation** - Intrinsic motivation research gets weaponized against workers
3. **The cycle is self-reinforcing** - Fear of worklessness drives overwork; overwork creates more precarity
4. **Charismatic leadership has dark origins** - German social science, Nietzschean overtones
5. **Personal branding corrupts relationships** - Treating yourself as a product undermines genuine connection

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Extends Baker's earlier essay** (in this file): This is the fuller argument he develops in his book
- **Complements Kevin Kelly's "Flounder Mode"**: Kelly offers an alternative—joyful work without suffering or personal brand anxiety
- **Relates to Brooks on ambition**: Both address the moral hazards of making work central to identity
- **Explains why self-help can't be discredited**: Economic precarity constantly renews demand for these ideas

## "Considerations" - Notes from the Underground, Revisited (notashelf.dev)

*Source: https://notashelf.dev/posts/considerations - Added: 2026-01-18*

A raw, introspective essay in the tradition of Dostoevsky's *Notes from Underground*. The author reflects on spite as a driving force, the paradox of accomplishment without satisfaction, and the tension between solitude and obligation.

### Spite as Fuel

The author identifies with Dostoevsky's underground man—not entirely, but in one key sentiment: spite.

> "I do things when I'm spiteful; I do things because I am spiteful, and it usually becomes a strange mixture of emotions, actions, and sentiments that gets me results."

Spite becomes a kind of engine—not healthy, perhaps, but functional. "Let it get worse" becomes a defiant mantra against physical pain and existential exhaustion.

### Mistrust and Self-Reliance

Trust, or rather mistrust, appears early:

> "Simply figuring out ways I can do things myself is more cost effective. Simply put, I don't trust. Not anyone but myself."

When stakes matter, the author operates alone—"surrounded by people whom I love, but alone, nevertheless."

### Accomplishment Without Satisfaction

Despite objective success ("I have accomplished things that people my age have not even dreamed of"), there's no pride:

> "I feel disappointed, but not unhappy. Never unhappy. How I feel cannot be described as unhappy, or depressed, or anything of that sort. Neither happy nor unhappy. Disappointed, at best."

The disappointment comes from seeing the trajectory clearly: knowing how things will end up in 5, 10 years and "pushing onwards as if there is something on the horizon."

### The Trap of Others' Trust

The author has invested time and effort, gaining others' trust in return. But this creates a prison:

> "I feel powerless to betray the trust people have put in me, a trust I have never shown them."

The drowning metaphor: "Every single cell in your body screams and pushes for one more moment of survival. Every nanosecond becomes minutes until it ends."

### Solitude as Liberation

> "I enjoy solitude, possibly more than anything else. More than the company of people I love."

The dream: living in the middle of nowhere with not a single soul around. But even that wouldn't satisfy the unnamed yearning—"It is not solitude, nor company. It is not some kind of big win... I want an end."

### The Self-Made Prison

The essay's central irony:

> "I built a prison, got inside, and locked myself in. I could get out whenever I wanted to, but I don't want to be wanting to escape."

The desire isn't for escape itself, but for permission to give up without consequences—"Let me stop grasping for air, and I will be at peace."

### The Cyclical Pattern

Nothing will change. Spite will find a new target, interest will distract from the meaninglessness:

> "Each time with more water in my lungs than the last, each time more aware of the inevitable. This is how I have been doing things for a while now, and it is probably going to remain that way until the end of time."

### Why This Matters

This isn't a self-help essay offering solutions. It's an honest articulation of a particular kind of high-achieving despair—successful by external measures, hollow inside. The author explicitly rejects the framing of "unhappy" or "depressed," finding those labels inadequate.

The essay serves as a counterpoint to the optimistic frameworks elsewhere in this file:
- Where Brooks offers "struggles" to wrestle with, this author has surrendered to spite
- Where Kelly finds joy in work, this author finds only obligation
- Where Baker critiques Stoicism for political quietism, this author embodies the acceptance that leads nowhere

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Dostoevsky's *Notes from Underground***: Direct inspiration—the "sick, spiteful, unattractive man"
- **The "trapped by trust" paradox**: Echoes Baker's observation about "mutual obligation" but experienced as burden rather than possibility
- **Success without fulfillment**: Relates to Brooks's warning about ambition emerging from "need love" rather than "gift love"
- **The 60/90 rule reversed**: If that essay warns against over-detachment, this author demonstrates what under-detachment looks like—too attached to others' expectations while detached from any sense of meaning

## "You Could Just Choose Optimism" - Quarter Mile

*Source: https://quarter--mile.com/You-Could-Just-Choose-Optimism - Added: 2026-01-18*

A short essay contrasting two approaches to life through the metaphor of airline passengers—one who complains about everything, one who finds joy in the same situations.

### The G vs. J Framework

The author presents two archetypes:
- **G** (grouchy): Complains about every detail—the flight, the service, the weather
- **J** (jolly): Finds delight in the same situations

The core insight: "There is nothing forcing you to complain. You could just choose optimism."

### Why Complaining Persists

People justify complaints through "standards"—believing they must vocalize dissatisfaction to maintain high expectations. But the author argues this creates a false dilemma: you can maintain standards without constant complaints.

### Agency Over Victimhood

Many blame external circumstances (housing markets, airlines, etc.) for unhappiness. The counter: accepting "response-ability"—assigning yourself authority over your reactions—transforms outcomes.

### Emotional Nuance

Importantly, optimism doesn't deny real pain. You can feel sadness about loss while refusing to use tragedy as justification for permanent grouchiness.

### Key Insight

> "Choosing optimism isn't naive positivity—it's recognizing that your interpretation of events shapes your experience, and you possess genuine agency in that interpretation."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: Both reject extremes—this piece rejects permanent grouchiness, that piece rejects permanent detachment. Both allow for feeling bad while choosing how to respond.
- **Complements Baker's critique**: Baker argues collective action expands your sphere of control; this piece argues individual interpretation does too—different mechanisms, complementary insights.
- **Tension with Kevin Kelly's "Flounder Mode"**: Kelly's joy emerges from following interests; this piece suggests joy can be chosen independent of circumstances.
- **Echoes the "Clearly Incorrect" epistemics**: Both emphasize personal accountability—one for truth, one for attitude.

## "How to Live an Intellectually Rich Life" (Utsav Mamoria, Substack)

*Source: https://utsavmamoria.substack.com/p/how-to-live-an-intellectually-rich - Added: 2026-01-18*

A framework for cultivating intellectual richness using allegorical storytelling (fantasy world of Vielmere) combined with practical advice. Draws on Conway's Game of Life, Dorothy Hodgkin, Yitang Zhang, and Paul Erdős.

### Core Framework

Four "tools" for the intellectual journey:

1. **Axe of Satisfaction** - Cut through consumerist distraction
2. **Torch of Curiosity** - Illuminate the caves of ignorance
3. **Oars of Routine** - Navigate the river of responsibilities
4. **Climbing Down from Your Peak** - Abandon expertise to become a student again

### Epistemic Anxiety

> "Epistemic Anxiety is the feeling of uneasiness, tension, and concern when you want to know the truth. You worry that your knowledge is incomplete and full of errors."

This resonates with Baker's critique: we feel this anxiety but often address it through individual coping rather than collective action or genuine inquiry.

### Ideas Need Diversity and Abundance (Game of Life)

Using Conway's Game of Life as metaphor:
- Too few ideas → your ideas die (Still Lifes)
- Too many similar ideas → you recycle the same ideas (Oscillators)
- Diverse, abundant ideas → emergence and self-organization

> "Our ideas need to be diverse. Our ideas need to be abundant."

### The Dorothy Hodgkin Model: Curiosity Across Domains

Hodgkin went from studying pebbles in streams → X-ray crystallography → structure of penicillin, B12, and insulin. Key pattern:
- Childhood curiosity (mineral analysis kit, age ~10)
- Pivotal book at 16 on X-ray crystallography
- 35-year persistence on insulin problem

> "All driven by wielding the Torch of Curiosity."

### The Yitang Zhang Model: Power of Routine

Zhang, a former Subway accountant, proved a major result on prime gaps (bounded gaps between primes, upper bound 70 million—infinitely better than infinity).

His approach:
- Same hours, same place, every day for years
- Rode school shuttles, walked corridors, thought
- Methodically checked each step ("very boring")
- Never stopped reading journals, even in obscurity

> "Consistency over Flashes of Brilliance. Repeatability over Spontaneous Genius."

### The Paul Erdős Model: Collaborative Expertise

Erdős collaborated with 500+ mathematicians, creating the "Erdős number" concept:
- Erdős = 0
- Direct collaborators = 1
- Their collaborators = 2
- ~90% of active mathematicians have Erdős number < 8

He believed math was fundamentally social. Would show up at colleagues' doors saying "my brain is open." Lived from a suitcase, donated earnings.

> "I do things when I'm spiteful" - but channeled into 1,500 published papers.

### Practical Advice: Finding Collaborators

1. Define 1-2 areas you want to learn about
2. Ask everyone: "Do you know someone who knows a lot about Y?"
3. Get warm introductions
4. Ask thoughtful questions; request learning paths, not explanations
5. Put in the hours; update them on progress
6. Repeat for life

### Climbing Down from Your Peak

To truly learn something new:
- Abandon your views about it (the expert's prison)
- Open space for contemplation
- Isolate yourself from your ego

Thomas Szasz: "Every act of conscious learning requires the willingness to suffer an injury to one's self-esteem."

### Documenting the Journey

> "Writing is emergence, of the ideas and the self."

The act of writing forces clear thinking, reveals connections, makes the intellectual journey visible to yourself and others.

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to Baker's critique**: Both value action over acceptance, but this piece focuses on intellectual action (learning, collaborating) rather than political action
- **Complements Kevin Kelly's "Flounder Mode"**: Both advocate following interests with joy; Erdős adds the collaboration dimension
- **Echoes learning frameworks**: The routine emphasis aligns with deliberate practice; the collaboration emphasis aligns with Karpathy's "beer talk" insight
- **Extends the "Too Ambitious" piece**: Erdős demonstrates quantity over quality in action—1,500 papers, not one perfect opus

## "Self Unhelped" - Psychoanalysis vs. Therapeutic Culture (Sydney Review of Books)

*Source: https://sydneyreviewofbooks.com/reviews/self-unhelped - Added: 2026-01-18*

Christian R. Gelder reviews books by Bruce Fink (*Miss-ing*) and Darian Leader (*Is It Ever Just Sex?*), exploring how psychoanalysis challenges both conventional therapy and the "therapy-positive" culture of skill-building and emotional management.

### The Climate of Therapy

Freud's language permeates everyday speech ("acting defensively," "feeling conflicted"), yet contemporary therapy is decidedly non-Freudian:

> "Today, however, therapy is more cognitively- and behaviourally-minded than psychoanalytic, and the therapeutic encounter is usually limited to ten to fifteen sessions."

The shift came from mid-century American psychology—Aaron Beck (cognitive therapy) and Albert Ellis (rational-emotive behavioral therapy). Ellis's approach emphasized individual responsibility and "taking control":
- His book *A Guide to Rational Living* had chapters titled "The Art of Never Being Desperately Unhappy" and "How to Stop Blaming and Start Living"
- Famous anecdote: challenged himself to approach 130 women at the Bronx Botanical Gardens to overcome social anxiety. Result: "Nobody vomited and ran away. Nobody called the cops."

A psychoanalytic response: "such behavioural signs—not vomiting and running away or calling the cops—are hardly inconsistent with deeper feelings of discomfort."

### Why CBT Dominates

Economic rationalization favors efficient therapies:
> "Ellis himself once symptomatically drew a parallel between his own practice and the psychoanalytic model along exactly these lines: 'Freud had a gene for inefficiency, and I think I have a gene for efficiency... Had I not been a therapist, I would have been an efficiency expert.'"

"Evidence-based" claims for CBT are complicated:
- A recent meta-analysis found CBT efficacy for depression *wanes over time*
- New therapeutic modalities may work like placebos—initial boost, then diminishing potency
- Nancy McWilliams: the demand for "fully efficient, completely scientific therapy" can itself be a marker of psychopathology (obsessional neurosis)

### Psychoanalysis as Counter-Cultural

Analysis "leaves open a space for inefficiency in a world defined largely by demands for the very opposite":

> "The fact that it refuses the false gods of pseudo-scientific techniques and offers a space where each person can be listened to outside the framework of the service industry." (Darian Leader)

Lacan: "Analysis as a science is always a science of the particular." There's no formula, no handbook, no standard model—"the ethics of an analysis often stresses the uniqueness of each case."

### The Incredulous Practice

Psychoanalysis can seem like "an account of why the therapeutic encounter is liable to fail":
- The unconscious, transference, resistance, repetition compulsion, defense mechanisms—all explain why people find it hard to change
- "Psychoanalysis is a fundamentally incredulous activity and seeks to disrupt even the sturdiest narratives we tell ourselves about ourselves"
- Lacan: "The basic thing about analysis is that people finally realise that they've been talking nonsense at full volume for years"

Freud's famous line: "The point of psychoanalysis was to turn 'neurotic misery into ordinary unhappiness.'"

The question: "What future does ordinary unhappiness have in the happiness industry?"

### Bruce Fink on Not-Knowing

Fink's work (translator of Lacan, author of clinical introductions) emphasizes the virtues of not understanding too readily:

> "A Lacanian analyst should recognise that 'he does not know the why and wherefore of the analysand's actions, thoughts, feelings, fantasies, and symptoms. All he knows (like Socrates) is how to ask questions.'"

The analyst doesn't rush to give "warmth, sympathy, recommendations, advice, or obvious interpretations":
> "Instead, he holds open a space characterised by a lack of knowledge, a lack of answers, and this spurs the patient on to find his own."

On love in analysis: if the analyst has one true love, it's the unconscious itself. The analyst's desire to analyze must "take precedence over love for or hatred of the analysand."

The goal: converting the analysand's transferred feelings into "a love for their own unconscious."

### Darian Leader on Sexuality

Leader's *Is It Ever Just Sex?* challenges simplified notions of sexuality. When someone says "It didn't mean anything, it was just sex":
> "The negation of meaning actually implies an important set of meaning-making practices: separating sex and meaning could register 'a desperate defensive measure brought into play precisely because sex really does mean an awful lot.'"

Sexuality is forged in childhood, alongside "the confusions, prohibitions and permissions that mark other developmental relations":
> "Aspects of parent-child interaction are less a sanctioning of sexuality than the actual creation of sexuality."

Children first learn of sex as "penetrating" and violent—"why on earth would we be so drawn to it later on... If we have a choice, why do we do it?"

### Sexual Scripts

Leader draws on Gagnon and Simon's concept of sexual scripts (cultural, interpersonal, intrapsychic):

> "If we have spent years fantasising about a particular person, and then, away on a trip, we return to our hotel room to find them naked waiting for us, we are more likely to call the police than to become sexually aroused. This is because the right script is not being followed."

Scripts shape when and how we have sex—even cultural details like clapping during sex (common in 17th century, "weird turn-off" today).

### Why We Have Sex at All

Given sexuality is forged alongside "anxiety, stress, pain, fear, and the negativity and absence wrought by the other," it's remarkable people manage to have sex:
> "Should 'the actual ability to perform sex' not itself be seen 'as a disorder'?"

The answer lies in power asymmetries:
> "If in sex we are playing out, pursuing and avenging many aspects of the early relationship with our caregivers, there will always be an imbalance of power... Yet in sex there is almost always the sense of reversing helplessness, as we are momentarily the cause of feelings and sensations in the other person."

### The Political Stakes

On Lacanian insularity vs. broader dialogue:
> "The real nightmare for most Lacanians is that Lacan's name will become just another reference in a list of footnotes—a polytheistic catastrophe that must be averted at all costs, with the unfortunate result that we risk losing out on everything we have to learn from American colleagues."

### Key Insight

> "It's still hard to talk about sexuality, and still no easier to talk to others about ourselves. And so Fink and Leader's books embody what is so singular about the Lacanian spirit, taking what cannot be said and putting it into the symbolic, keeping lack in the picture in a culture where lack seems to be so profoundly lacking."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Directly challenges CBT/positive psychology**: Where Baker critiques self-help ideology, this piece critiques the therapeutic apparatus itself—the demand for efficiency, skill-building, and measurable outcomes
- **Echoes Baker on inefficiency**: Both argue that something valuable is lost when we optimize for measurable results
- **Relates to "It's Okay to Feel Bad"**: Both push back against the pathologizing of ordinary unhappiness—psychoanalysis explicitly aims for it
- **Complements Kevin Kelly**: Kelly offers joyful work without suffering; psychoanalysis offers meaning through attending to suffering rather than fixing it
- **The "lack" insight**: In a culture obsessed with self-improvement and completion, psychoanalysis insists on fundamental incompleteness—"human beings cannot help but consider themselves to be nothing more than beings who are, in the end, missing something"

## "What is your personal totalizing and self-consistent worldview/philosophy?" (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/yfoJe8eiHyLuNzdES/what-is-your-personal-totalizing-and-self-consistent - Added: 2026-01-18*

A template for articulating a personal philosophy, in the tradition of Paul Graham, Eliezer Yudkowsky, and others who develop "totalizing and self-consistent worldviews."

### The Problem: Evolution's Orthogonal Values

The core argument: evolution is an optimizer with a target ("inclusive genetic fitness") fundamentally orthogonal to human values.

**Where values align with evolution:**
- You want good eyesight → evolution wants you to have good eyesight

**Where values diverge:**
- You want to suffer less → evolution doesn't care about suffering
- Evolution would "happily torture you and a million other of its children for a million years" for a 0.001% fitness increase

### The Resulting Horrors

Because evolution optimizes for fitness, not welfare:
- **Genocide and factory farms** are "good" from evolution's perspective (instrumentally useful)
- But **compassion** is also instrumentally useful
- Putting both into the same brain creates "a mess of contradictions incompatible with sanity"

> "This isn't just misinformation in your environment. It's part of the genetically-programmed biophysical architecture of your brain."

### The Simulation Problem

A second core distortion: your brain creates a real-time simulation of your environment.

- This simulation *feels* like external physical reality
- But it exists only within "the tiny slice of physical reality that is your brain"
- Evolution *doesn't want* you to notice this distinction
- It wants you to believe the simulation reflects external reality

### Memetic Amplification

Nations, religions, products, and other competitive memeplexes "hijack evolution's distortions and make them 1,000× worse."

### The Way Out

Despite these distortions, every memeplex must have "some accurate sense of the truth to not be outcompeted":

> "It may be warped and distorted, but it's there. And if you seize on that kernel of truth, protect it, nurture it, and be forever vigilant that you, yourself, may have been corrupted, then it will grow."

This is framed as "the ultimate battle inside of every conscious being and every unconscious thinking machine."

### The Call to Action

The author invites readers to articulate their own worldviews:
- What are your most important Litanies?
- What are your n noble truths?

Influenced by: Paul Graham, Isaac Asimov, Joel Spolsky, Brett McKay, Shakyamuni, Chuck Palahniuk, Bryan Caplan, qntm, Eliezer Yudkowsky.

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to Baker's critique of acceptance**: Both suggest that passive acceptance is insufficient—active engagement with truth is required
- **Echoes "Clearly Incorrect"**: Both emphasize personal vigilance about truth and corruption
- **Extends evolutionary psychology frameworks**: Takes the "misalignment" framing from AI safety and applies it to human nature itself
- **Complements the psychoanalytic view**: Both acknowledge that our perceptions are systematically distorted—psychoanalysis by childhood, this by evolution
- **The rationalist tradition**: This is the LessWrong/Yudkowsky school of thought—explicit about worldview construction, vigilance against bias, and treating truth as a practice

## "Tragic Optimism: How to Keep Going Amid the Chaos" (Brad Stulberg, NYT)

*Source: https://www.nytimes.com/2024/11/29/opinion/tragic-optimism.html - Added: 2026-01-18*

Viktor Frankl's concept of "tragic optimism" applied to contemporary crises—Hurricane Helene, political despair—as a framework for resilience that avoids both toxic positivity and debilitating pessimism.

### The Core Concept

Viktor Frankl (Holocaust survivor, psychologist) coined "tragic optimism" in 1949. It's built on three universal tragedies:

1. **Pain** - we are made of flesh and bone
2. **Guilt** - we have freedom to choose and thus feel responsible
3. **Loss** - everything we cherish is impermanent, including our lives

Tragic optimism means:
> "Acknowledging, accepting and even expecting that life will contain hardship and hurt, then doing everything we can to move forward with a positive attitude anyway."

### The Third Way

Stulberg identifies two dysfunctional extremes:
- **Toxic positivity**: Denies anything is wrong → nothing to change
- **Excessive pessimism**: Says action is pointless → paralysis

Between them:
> "Committing to wise hope and wise action."

Wise hope and wise action:
- Accept the situation clearly for what it is
- Focus on what you can control
- Remember you've faced challenges before
- Continue because standing still isn't an option

### Emotional Flexibility Research

Post-9/11 study of college students:
- Those who held hope *alongside* loss showed greater resilience
- They still experienced anger, fear, sadness
- The difference: they could hold positive emotions simultaneously

This isn't denial—it's emotional flexibility:
> "Giving us permission to feel happiness and sadness, hope and fear, loss and possibility — sometimes in the same day, and even in the same hour."

### The Agency Loop

A key insight:
> "Recognizing that we maintain agency fuels hope, and maintaining hope reminds us that we have agency."

Resilience factors:
- Leaning into community
- Self-compassion
- Small routines for mental health
- Feeling sadness while maintaining hope
- Commitment to productive action

### What It's Not

Tragic optimism does NOT:
- Encourage seeking out suffering
- Claim everything must be meaningful
- Romanticize hardship

Sometimes things just suck. The framework acknowledges this while insisting we have *some* say in how we face it.

### Author Context

Brad Stulberg wrote *Master of Change* and writes about mental health and performance. Writing from western North Carolina after Hurricane Helene—concrete, lived experience of community resilience.

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Directly complements "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: Both reject extremes (over-detachment vs. wallowing), both allow negative emotions while maintaining forward motion
- **Echoes Baker's "wise action" call**: Baker argues for collective action; Stulberg focuses on individual/community resilience—both reject passive acceptance
- **Relates to "You Could Just Choose Optimism"**: That piece is simpler (just choose joy); Stulberg's framework is more nuanced—you can't always choose joy, but you can choose wise action
- **Tension with "Considerations" (Dostoevsky piece)**: That author is trapped in spite and accomplishment without satisfaction; tragic optimism offers a potential path out through agency and community
- **Frankl vs. Epictetus**: Frankl's framework is warmer than Stoic detachment—it doesn't ask you to rehearse your child's death, just to hold hope alongside loss

## "Channel Mapping: Motivation as Flow" (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/wzd7RWNZFg4zRJNz6/what-if-we-could-map-our-motivation-as-channels-of-flow - Added: 2026-01-18*

A framework for systematizing motivation by mapping "Peak Motivation Moments" (PMMs) and designing channels to recreate them—drawing on Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory, dynamic systems, and (improbably) Miyamoto Musashi's irrigation techniques.

### The Core Metaphor

Musashi learned to farm by observation: rather than building barriers against water, he designed channels to direct flow. The lesson:

> "True mastery—whether in battle or in life—comes not from imposing one's will upon the world, but from understanding and working with its natural flows."

Applied to motivation: don't fight your energy patterns; channel them.

### The System

**1. Peak Motivation Moments (PMMs)**
- Rare instances when you feel "fully aligned, energized, and purposeful"
- Serve as calibration points—your "optimal state of performance"
- Reference points for what's possible

**2. Channel Mapping (CM)**
- Dual-axis system:
  - X-Axis (Internal): Self-development, mindset, energy management
  - Y-Axis (External): Environment, support systems, timing
- If PMM is the destination, CM is the map and engineering

**3. The 10-Unit Exercise**
- Imagine recreating your PMM with 10 units of energy
- How many units go to internal changes? External adjustments?
- Example: "7 units internal (confidence, skills) + 3 external (classroom setup)"

### Theoretical Grounding

Claims to integrate:
- **Flow theory** (Csikszentmihalyi): Skill-challenge balance, focused attention
- **Dynamic systems**: Feedback loops, emergence
- **Neuroplasticity**: Creating sustainable habits
- **Behavioral psychology**: Reinforcement, goal-setting

### The Iterative Promise

> "Each new PMM you identify provides more refined data for your map."

Like a garden that improves with each season:
- First establish basic channels
- Observe how water flows
- Adjust based on results
- The system becomes more efficient over time

### Critical Assessment

**What it gets right:**
- The observation that motivation varies and can be studied
- The internal/external distinction (useful for diagnosis)
- The calibration idea—using your own peak moments as reference points

**What's questionable:**
- The "10 units" quantification seems arbitrary and unfalsifiable
- Heavy self-help jargon ("ESTIMAT system," "MetaVirtues")
- Relies on Csikszentmihalyi without engaging critiques (see "The Cult of Doing Business" entry—his work was co-opted into *Good Business: Leadership, Flow, and the Making of Meaning*)
- The Musashi story is apocryphal (prefaced with "at one point in his life"—no source)

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Relates to Brooks's ambition framework**: Both attempt to systematize motivation, but Brooks focuses on moral wrestling while this focuses on mechanical optimization
- **Tension with Baker's critique**: This is exactly the kind of individual-focused system that Baker argues distracts from structural analysis—"turning chaos into manageable streams of effort" is a Band-Aid if the chaos is externally manufactured
- **Echoes "Too Ambitious" piece**: Both reference the skill-challenge balance, but the Maalvika piece focuses on action over planning, while this is a planning framework
- **Relates to psychoanalysis critique**: Fink would ask: what are you defending against by systematizing motivation? The framework assumes conscious access to your own patterns—psychoanalysis is skeptical
- **The "efficiency" question**: This is the kind of self-optimization Nancy McWilliams might call "a marker of obsessional neurosis"—the demand for a fully efficient, completely scientific approach to motivation

## "You Are Not Too 'Irrational' to Know Your Preferences" (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/LifRBXdenQDiX4cu8/you-are-not-too-irrational-to-know-your-preferences-1 - Added: 2026-01-18*

A rationalist therapist's defense of preferences against weaponized "rationality." The author (13 years as therapist, 7 years teaching at rationality camps) argues that preferences and wants are fundamentally orthogonal to rationality—and critiques how "rational" has become a tool for manipulation.

### The Core Argument

Rationality properly refers to:
- **Epistemic rationality**: Minimizing false beliefs
- **Instrumental rationality**: Actions that maximize goal fulfillment

What it does NOT apply to: preferences, wants, feelings, or experiential qualia.

> "Wants are emergent, complex forms of pain and pleasure. They are either felt or they are not felt, and reason only comes in at the stage of deciding what to do about them."

### The Ice Cream Example

When Ash wants ice cream and Bryce says "That's irrational":
- Pointing out downsides (sugar, weight, cost) is legitimate information-sharing
- Calling the *preference itself* irrational is a category error
- Preferences are not assertions that can be true or false—they're experiences

The author's position: "You want people to enjoy their ice cream even if it's 'irrational.' I want people to know that preferences are never 'irrational.' We are not the same."

### Four Key Arguments

**1. You are not too stupid to know what you want.**
- Preferences are independent from intelligence
- "Inconsistent preferences" only makes sense if you assume humans are monolithic entities
- Your parts/values can legitimately conflict without any of them being "irrational"
- "Stop judging your wants, and stop listening to other people who judge them"

**2. Feeling hurt is not a sign of irrationality.**
- Feelings are "lossy heuristics and blunt motivations"—not claims to be evaluated
- Being hurt by criticism is independent of whether the criticism is true
- Wielding "truth" like a mallet is fundamentally hostile, regardless of intent
- High-school negging dressed up as rationality is still manipulation

**3. Illegible preferences are not invalid.**
- Preferences don't need to be explained or justified to be valid
- Legibility is for planning and coordination, not justification
- "Because I don't want to" is always sufficient reason (though social consequences still apply)
- If someone tries to use "reason" to shame a preference, they're probably manipulating you

**4. Your preferences don't need to match your community's.**
- Every community has norms, but these shouldn't override your sense of right/wrong
- Red flag: "Don't ask outsiders, they wouldn't understand"
- If someone claims you're "not rational enough" to disagree, check how representative they are
- "Why on earth should you give up on your preferences?"

### The Manipulation Pattern

The author identifies a specific abuse dynamic:

> "If they try to justify it as a matter of virtue, or 'rationality?' If you ask them to stop and they start quoting the Litany of Gendlin at you and insisting that if you were smarter you'd be thankful that they're willing to honestly tell you how unattractive or stupid you are? That's not rationality."

This pattern appears in:
- Romantic relationships (title alternative: "Red flags if dating rationalists")
- Family dynamics
- Intellectual subcultures
- Two-person dynamics ("Two people are enough to form a cult")

### The Orthogonality Thesis for Humans

The frame treats values/preferences as entirely separate from "rational" capability—similar to the AI orthogonality thesis (goals vs. capability-to-achieve-goals).

Alternative frame: our preferences, being products of natural selection and conditioning, are "fully rational expressions of our genetic predispositions given the complexity and superstimuli of the modern world."

Either way: no value-gain in judging preferences by intelligence.

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Preferences ≠ assertions** - They can't be "wrong" the way beliefs can
2. **Emotional hurt ≠ evidence of irrationality** - Hurt comes from feeling unseen, not from inaccuracy
3. **Illegibility ≠ invalidity** - You don't owe anyone an explanation for what you want
4. **Community pressure ≠ rationality** - Neither a whole community nor a whole world can make your preferences "irrational"
5. **"Because I don't want to" is sufficient** - Social consequences exist, but the preference itself is valid

### Key Quote

> "I'm gatekeeping the term [rationality] away from that sort of high-school negging bullshit."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Complements "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: Both defend the legitimacy of feelings against over-rationalization—that piece defends negative emotions, this one defends preferences
- **Echoes Baker's critique**: Both identify how "rational" language can be weaponized—Baker on self-help, this on interpersonal manipulation
- **Relates to psychoanalysis entry**: Fink's emphasis on not rushing to interpret echoes here—the analyst doesn't tell you your feelings are wrong
- **Extends "Clearly Incorrect"**: Both critique misuse of authority—that piece on expertise, this one on "rationality"
- **Tension with Channel Mapping**: That framework assumes conscious access to motivation patterns; this piece would say your preferences are valid whether or not they're mappable
- **The cult warning**: "Two people are enough to form a cult"—weaponized rationality is a specific manipulation technology

## "Conscious Significance" - A Framework Between Free Will and Determinism (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/54pbgmxiBRNaYEqMq/implications-how-conscious-significance-could-inform-our - Added: 2026-01-18*

A philosophical framework for understanding autonomy that is determinism-agnostic. The author argues that "conscious significance" maintains what matters about autonomy without requiring libertarian free will—our conscious effort is significant to determining outcomes, even if determinism is true.

### The Core Framework

**Conscious Significance**: Acting with the understanding that our conscious effort matters for determining outcomes—not claiming "free will" but recognizing that deliberate thought and effort influence what happens.

This perspective overlaps with Eliezer Yudkowsky's "Requiredism" and attempts to:
- Maintain scientific integrity (compatible with determinism)
- Preserve important norms around responsibility
- Avoid the naive determinist trap ("nothing matters because I have no choice")

### What Remains Unchanged Under Determinism

**Consequences still matter**: Determinism *guarantees* consequences and makes them foreseeable. We don't step into traffic because we intuit deterministic physics. Conscious significance means we actively simulate actions and predict outcomes.

**Past is prologue**: If someone consistently exhibits harmful behavior, it's reasonable to expect similar future behavior—regardless of whether they "chose" those patterns. Behavioral patterns indicate underlying motivations.

**We are part of others' environments**: Granting determinism, our responses to others' behavior form part of the environment that shapes their future choices. Intervention is justified as environmental influence.

### The "Positive Double Standard"

Key insight: We cannot know how much conscious effort someone else is putting in. A homeless person may be struggling psychologically just to survive; an Olympic athlete may be driven by compulsion rather than heroic effort.

> "Personal responsibility is just that—personal."

The double standard:
- **Hold yourself accountable** for your level of conscious effort (you know what you're actually putting in)
- **Don't hold others accountable** for their effort level (you can't know it)

This doesn't mean ignoring behavioral patterns—it means distinguishing between "this person's behavior creates negative outcomes" and "this person isn't trying hard enough."

### Implications

**Prison reform**: Data-driven, not ideology-driven. Rehabilitation can work (because conscious significance allows learning and change), but the approach should be empirically tested, not derived from metaphysical commitments. The author notes that some meditation programs backfire in prisons due to the nuances of traumatized brains.

**Guilt vs. Shame**: Drawing on Brené Brown:
- **Guilt** (useful): "I did something bad" → simulating alternatives → informing future choices
- **Shame** (harmful): "I am bad" → reinforcing negative identity → more likely to repeat

> "While a naive interpretation of determinism might suggest we should do away with guilt, what we want to do away with is actually shame."

The domestic abuser who focuses on guilt may repair relationships; the one who focuses on shame reinforces their identity as an abuser.

**Political alignment**: The framework is "left-leaning" in the sense that:
- Environment needs to change for populations to change (not vice versa)
- We can't expect people to "pull themselves up by their bootstraps"
- But individual autonomy and capacity for change remain intact

### Autonomy Isn't Always the Goal

Low-autonomy states can be valuable:
- Being a reliable driver, parent, or dieter
- "Flow" states in creative work
- Habits that serve you well

But these states are rarely memorable. Engaging deliberately makes experiences more vivid and present. The tension: do we want to be present, or do we want time to pass efficiently?

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Directly addresses the acceptance vs. action tension**: Unlike naive Stoicism ("accept what you can't control"), conscious significance maintains agency while accepting determinism may be true
- **Echoes Baker's collective responsibility insight**: "We are part of others' environments" parallels Baker's "things true in the first-person plural"
- **Relates to "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: Both allow negative emotions to be functional—guilt motivates change, just as fear calibrates risk
- **Extends Brooks's ambition framework**: Both acknowledge mixed motives; conscious significance provides a determinism-compatible foundation
- **Complements the psychoanalysis critique**: Both reject the CBT/efficiency framing—change happens through engagement, not technique
- **Provides foundation for "You Are Not Too Irrational"**: If preferences are orthogonal to rationality, and effort level is personal, then judging others' effort/preferences is doubly unwarranted

## "Do Hard Things Carefully" - The Edge Framework (Patrick Maguire)

*Source: https://blog.depthsofrepair.com/p/do-hard-things-carefully - Added: 2026-01-18*

A framework for navigating challenges that offers a middle path between "push through" and "listen to your gut."

### The Problem with Simple Advice

Neither extreme has felt right to the author:

**"Push through"** has merit—occasional 12-hour days to meet deadlines haven't led to regret. But the author wishes he'd stopped to ask for help while working in Sudan instead of pretending everything was fine for months.

**"Listen to your gut"** has been right sometimes, but the gut also constantly urges staying home instead of going to parties, or not posting writing online. The body contains wisdom, but it's also trying to protect against being eaten by lions—not a modern concern.

### The Edge Concept

> "My edge is the zone in which I feel like I'm challenging myself, where I'm outside of my comfort zone, but not so far outside of it that I risk doing myself harm."

It's a point of balance between hard and achievable.

**The Hike Example**:
- Comfort zone: Complete in 70 minutes
- Risk of injury: Aim for 45 minutes
- **Edge**: Aim for 57 minutes—a stretch goal within possibility
- But if recovering from injury or feeling sick, the edge might just be completing the hike at all

The crucial insight: **your edge is not a constant, but a constantly shifting zone** based on context.

### Context-Dependence

The same person at different events has different edges:

**At a new friend's dinner party (feeling anxious):**
- Edge might be just introducing yourself to everyone instead of staying in the kitchen

**Hosting a dinner party with close friends:**
- Edge is much further—maybe having real moments of connection with everyone, not burning food, AND staying aware of the room

The more connected and safe you feel, the more you have to stretch to reach that challenging zone.

### The Edge and Vulnerability

Especially valuable when vulnerability is involved:

**Scenario**: Coffee with a new acquaintance who asks about something tender.

Check in: Does sharing feel a little risky but generally okay? Or am I not yet in a place of feeling safe enough?

Factors that shift the edge:
- Feeling really connected that day
- Being exhausted
- Not knowing enough about the person yet
- Having had an emotionally taxing conversation the day before

### Tracking Your Growing Capacity

One benefit of tracking your edge: recognizing your own growing ability to be vulnerable.

**Example**: Talking about alcohol addiction used to require constant edge-checking—is this person likely to shame me? Today, the author finds the edge so far out it's barely visible. He can talk about it with minimal risk of regret.

### Rubber-Banding

> "Practicing tracking your edge allows you to engage in rubber-banding—stretching yourself while still being able to snap back."

Without keeping your edge in mind:
- You may stretch to the point of breaking
- Or stretch so little that you never grow

### Practical Toolbox

1. When feeling discomfort, slow down and take a deep breath
2. Check in: Where is my edge right now?
3. What level of discomfort feels challenging but not overwhelming?
4. Can I lean in? Or have I already leaned in too far?
5. Act accordingly, and keep checking as the situation progresses
6. Afterward, check in again—how did that go?
7. Give yourself credit and watch for the vulnerability hangover

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Complements "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: Both reject extremes and allow for discomfort while maintaining agency. The edge framework provides a practical mechanism for calibrating how much discomfort to take on.
- **Related to Brooks's ambition framework**: Both involve wrestling with internal tensions rather than resolving them. The edge is dynamic, like Brooks's "internal struggles."
- **Echoes the Local Optimality insight**: The edge shifts based on current constraints—just as chronic issues are locally optimal given current resources, the edge represents what's achievable given current emotional/physical state.
- **Tension with "push through" culture**: The edge framework explicitly rejects the idea that more challenge is always better—context matters.
- **Authentic Relating connection**: The author learned these concepts through Authentic Relating practice, which emphasizes relational safety and vulnerability.

## "Rawls the Redeemer" - Liberalism as an Ethical Framework (Alexandre Lefebvre, Aeon)

*Source: https://aeon.co/essays/john-rawls-liberalism-and-what-it-means-to-live-a-good-life - Added: 2026-01-19*

A reframing of John Rawls's political philosophy as an ethical/spiritual framework for finding meaning. The author (professor of politics and philosophy, author of *Liberalism as a Way of Life*) argues that liberalism can and should be understood as a comprehensive worldview for the unchurched—not just a legal-political doctrine.

### The Hidden Question

In unpublished notes from 1991, Rawls asked himself:

> "Does life need to be redeemed? And if so, why; and what can redeem it? I would say yes: life does need to be redeemed."

By "redeemed" he meant the ordinary round of life—growing up, falling in love, having children, growing older—needs to be "graced by something" to be worthwhile. The question is what provides that grace.

Rawls lost his faith as a soldier in WWII but never abandoned the conviction that life needs elevation. The author argues Rawls found that elevation in liberalism.

### The Source of Modern Values

The author's key question for secular people:

> "If you, like me, are unchurched and don't draw your values from a religion, then where do you get them from?"

Common answers—"from experience," "from friends and family," "from human nature"—are insufficient. These are all situated within wider contexts. What's the society-or-civilisation-sized thing that shapes who we are?

The argument: **liberalism** (with values of personal freedom, fairness, reciprocity, tolerance, and irony) is that thing for most people in Western liberal democracies—not just politically but in "all walks of life, from the family to the workplace, from friendship to enmity, from humour to outrage."

### Why Liberalism's Ethical Core Vanished

19th-century founders (Constant, de Staël, Tocqueville, Eliot, Mill) conceived liberalism first as an ethical project—a "moral adventure" for living well in the modern world.

The word "liberal" combines freedom (liberty) and generosity (liberality). The founding question: How can ordinary people remain free *and* generous despite capitalism's consumerism, democracy's conformism, and nationalism's partiality?

But liberalism retreated to a purely legal/political doctrine. Causes include:
- Early 20th-century "classical liberalism" (reacting to New Deal progressivism)
- Cold War liberals repudiating their predecessors' progressivism
- Contemporary "political liberalism" (ironically founded by Rawls's 1993 work) that eschews questions of the good life

### The Defense Failure

Liberals are "awful at defending themselves":
- Crisis discussions fixate on liberalism's opponents (populists, authoritarians)
- When defended, the reasons are almost exclusively institutional (rule of law, individual rights)
- The spiritual/existential reasons to care are never articulated

### Fairness as the Core

Rawls's central idea: society should be conceived as a **fair system of cooperation**. This isn't his invention—he claims citizens of liberal democracies already affirm this implicitly. Public institutions profess to advance freedom, dignity, and equal opportunity.

The author notes Rawls's best interpreters report feeling déjà vu—recognition of what they already believe.

### The Original Position

Rawls's famous thought experiment:
- Imagine selecting principles of justice from behind a "veil of ignorance"
- You don't know your sex, gender, class, race, religion, etc.
- Which principles would you pick?

The answer: those favoring fairness in rights, self-respect, resources, and opportunities.

**The Ernie and Jack Example**:

Rawls's autobiography recounts a formative experience as a privileged Princeton student working in a doughnut factory alongside Ernie, a man resigned to 12-hour days breathing flour dust for 18 years.

- **Ernie** has nothing to lose behind the veil—fair principles help him
- **Jack** (the princeling) must ask why he should bracket his advantages

### What's in It for Jack?

Two answers:

1. **It's the right thing to do** - morally correct

2. **Great spiritual goods come from living up to liberal principles**:
   - **Impartiality** - liberating yourself from narrow self-interest
   - **Autonomy** - acting according to values, free from coercion
   - **Resilience** - moral fortitude in the face of temptation
   - **Grace** - fulfilling requirements of justice with pleasure and ease

### The Redemption

Liberalism has no metaphysics—no soul, no Great Beyond.

> "Pfffftt," goes the liberal.

But liberals never gave up on the core of religion: seeking meaning through something beyond ourselves. The liberal "Beyond" is worldly—an ideal of becoming a free and generous person in a fair and just society.

> "Redemption is not found only in a liberal way of life. Heaven forbid. Yet it's there too, waiting for liberals to answer its call."

### Key Insights

1. **Liberalism already is your worldview** - If unchurched in a liberal democracy, liberalism likely shapes who you are far more than you recognize

2. **The ethical dimension has been lost** - 19th-century founders saw it as moral adventure; 20th-century heirs reduced it to institutions

3. **Fairness isn't abstract** - It emerges from recognizing that positional advantages (like Jack's vs. Ernie's) shouldn't determine life outcomes

4. **The original position is a spiritual practice** - Not just a thought experiment but "a transformative spiritual practice" for cultivating impartiality

5. **The Beyond is immanent** - Liberal redemption doesn't require another plane of existence—it's found in striving toward ideals of fairness

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Extends Baker's critique**: Baker argues self-help philosophy is the "Janus face" of liberal capitalism; Lefebvre reclaims liberalism as something worth defending—not as self-help but as ethical framework. Both agree the current version is impoverished.

- **Complements "Tragic Optimism"**: Frankl's framework addresses how to find meaning amid suffering; Lefebvre's addresses where secular Westerners get their values from. Both answer "what redeems life?"

- **Relates to "The Cult of Doing Business"**: That piece traces how positive psychology got co-opted by entrepreneurship. Lefebvre argues something similar happened to liberalism—its ethical core was extracted, leaving only institutional shell.

- **Tension with psychoanalysis critique**: Psychoanalysis embraces "ordinary unhappiness"; Lefebvre's liberalism offers something more aspirational—but both reject the happiness industry's demands for optimization.

- **Echoes "Conscious Significance"**: Both frameworks are determinism-agnostic while preserving meaningful agency. Conscious significance says your effort matters; liberal ethics says your impartiality matters.

- **Related to Brooks on ambition**: Brooks's framework helps you be driven without being a jerk; Lefebvre's original position provides the moral foundation—bracket your advantages when thinking about justice.

### Why This Matters Now

The essay arrives amid widespread liberal malaise. Defenders of liberalism tend to argue instrumentally (institutions matter) rather than spiritually (this is who you are). Lefebvre's reframing offers an answer to the question secular people rarely ask themselves: where do my values actually come from?

## "Why Doesn't Advice Work?" (Dynomight)

*Source: https://dynomight.substack.com/p/advice - Added: 2026-01-19*

A taxonomy of reasons good advice gets ignored, using the Mahabharata as framing: Duryodhana ignores Krishna's (possibly divine) counsel and launches a war that kills 4 million. The author asks: "So why didn't they listen?"

### The Taxonomy of Advice Failure

**1. Maybe your advice is bad.**
- Median nutrition advice is appalling
- Kennedy's Joint Chiefs advised bombing Soviet missiles in Cuba
- Internet people recommend divorce for minor offenses
- But: people do ignore genuinely good advice too (they'll eventually explain your own advice back to you as novel)

**2. Maybe your advice is incomplete without lived experience.**
Climbing example: "Move your feet up" is good advice, but it only works once you've internalized dozens of other things through trial and error:
- Finding the right holds
- Grabbing them correctly
- Getting center of gravity positioned
- Twisting hips, knees, ankles into the right angles
- Moving the right foot at the right speed

> "Move your feet up" seems like a good explanation, because it's the most visible action. But it only works once you've internalized lots of other stuff.

**3. Maybe people don't understand it.**
The author reports personal revelations where he finally understood hints people had been dropping for years ("Wow you sure drink a lot of coffee!"). His brain had been converting their words into "a big whooshing sound."

**Possible cognitive bias:** Our brains often make us *not understand* advice we don't want to follow (e.g., someone who needs to end a relationship convincing themselves their situation is exceptional).

**4. Maybe they don't feel like it will work.**
**Jim Theory of procrastination:** A guy named Jim lives in your head. Before you try something, Jim calculates effort-to-benefit ratio. If he doesn't like it, he adds a "tax" making the thing very hard to do. Jim is stubborn and not very bright.

Example: A friend always wanted to program, had the skills, but couldn't transition careers because he never *felt* it could work. "It's incredibly hard to do things when Jim isn't on board."

**5. Maybe what works for you doesn't work for others.**
After the author's dog died, a friend recommended reality TV bingeing. But the author's "reality TV" is goat videos. People vary a lot, often along unsuspected dimensions.

**6. Maybe your advice requires too much willpower.**
The author's running advice has convinced exactly zero people to develop a long-term running habit. His air purifier advice has converted many people. Why? Running is hard. Buying an air purifier is easy.

**7. Maybe we don't care if advice is useful.**
Alternative reasons for seeking advice:
- **Validation:** Keep asking until someone confirms your predetermined choice
- **Conversation:** Advice-seeking is a great social move, especially with hypotheticals
- **Guardrail:** Making sure no one has irrefutable proof of catastrophe (even if we only change our minds 1% of the time)
- **Economic:** Advice is cheap, so we keep asking in increasingly unlikely-to-help situations until expected benefit matches tiny cost

**8. Maybe we're stuck inside our heads.**
Too much information, too close to the situation. The author spent months asking everyone what to do about a job change overseas. In retrospect, there was no solution—he just couldn't see it.

**9. Maybe when advice works, we don't call it "advice."**
Examples that work but we don't classify as advice:
- Avoid drinking pond water
- Do practice problems to get better at math
- Good "user experience" makes people want to interact with you more

**10. Maybe the reason advice is needed is the reason people can't follow it.**
The author struggles with email: "Why don't you just answer everything as it comes in?" If he could do that, he wouldn't have the problem in the first place.

> "Probably everyone has some issues and struggles to deal with some things. Maybe the same issues that make them need your advice also make them unable to use it."

### Practical Takeaways

**For consumers of advice:**
1. If respected people repeatedly give advice that doesn't make sense, consider you might be missing something
2. Maybe try blindly following advice sometimes? If you've repeatedly made terrible choices in some domain, try the wisdom of crowds instead

**For producers of advice:**
1. Have realistic expectations—most advice isn't followed
2. If you really want impact, focus on advice that is **easy to follow**

### The Punchline

> "So I guess that's my advice on advice? Embrace consumerism? Not what I expected."

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Directly relates to "Move your feet up" problem in learning frameworks**: The isolation principle—advice that seems complete often requires internalized prerequisites
- **Echoes Baker's critique**: Structural constraints make individual advice inadequate; "Jim Theory" is an individual-level version of manufactured precarity
- **Relates to Brooks on ambition**: The "doesn't feel like it will work" failure mode connects to Brooks's "need love" vs "gift love"—someone driven by need love may not believe in their own agency
- **Complements "It's Okay to Feel Bad for a Bit"**: That piece argues against over-processing emotions; this piece explains why even good emotional advice often fails
- **Tension with Kevin Kelly's "Flounder Mode"**: Kelly's success came from following interests joyfully; the author implies most people can't just follow good advice to do the same
- **Relates to psychoanalysis critique**: Fink's emphasis on "not knowing" aligns with the "incomplete without lived experience" point—advice is always partial

## "Complicated World: Human Life" - An Alternative Framework

*Source: https://complicated.world/en/Human_Life - Added: 2026-01-18*

A more thoughtful approach to happiness that explicitly rejects "positive thinking" shortcuts in favor of authenticity and self-reflection. Notably critiques the Stoic approach of muting all emotions.

Happiness is "the target state that we want to achieve, at which we would be in our ideal condition." It's entirely subjective—listening to feelings is the sole source for learning what your happiness is.

**Key distinction from mainstream self-help:**
> "The advice that we most often get is all about how to avoid negative emotions, stress and all kinds of problems and how to instead invite more positivity into our lives... In contrast, we believe that the foundation of seeking happiness is being true with ourselves. Instead of muting the negative emotions, we believe that we should listen to them carefully, as they are there only for our benefit."

### What Makes People Happy

**Being True (Authenticity)**
- The most crucial factor

- "If you roughly know what you want, you must take steps to move in that direction. It will never happen by itself."

> "A common mistake to sacrifice ourselves now in order to be happy in the future."

There's a difference between:
1. Hard work toward a true goal
2. Being unhappy now believing it will be rewarded later

> "We can be happy tomorrow. Tomorrow is gonna be that day. Tomorrow could even be right now, if it could be at all."

If you're unhappy and proud of it, you may be caught in this pattern. "This approach is addictive like a drug."

## The Self-Appreciation Practice (NYT Morning Newsletter)

*Source: https://www.nytimes.com/2024/06/29/briefing/how-to-like-yourself-more.html - Added: 2026-01-18*

A practical alternative to abstract self-help philosophy: daily journaling of what you appreciate about yourself.

### The Practice

At the end of each day, write down things you appreciate about yourself:
- How you handled a difficult situation
- Something witty you said
- Checking off a procrastinated task
- Reframing how you were thinking about something
- On sparse days: making the bed, hair looking good

### Why It Works (Contrasted with Generic Gratitude)

**Gratitude practice**: Focus on external things—kindness of strangers, how your child looks at you, smell of honeysuckle. Offers perspective by reminding you "you're not alone."

**Appreciation practice**: A "refraction" of gratitude toward yourself. Not the opposite of gratitude, but its inward turn.

> "It's expressing gratitude for oneself, which at first feels conceited, but eventually, for me, has come to seem anything but."

### The Mechanism

**Shifts the question**: Instead of the mind's default detective mode ("What did I do wrong? Could have done better? Left undone?"), you start with "What did I do right today?"

**Reveals patterns**: Over time, you notice positive behavioral patterns and effects on others. "And when you see that, you start to like yourself more."

**Changes behavior proactively**:
> "I've found myself behaving differently—more assertively, more compassionately—simply because I know that, tonight, I'll sit down and look at my day, and I know how good it will feel to appreciate these things about myself."

### Why This Avoids Pop-Stoicism Pitfalls

The practice isn't about:
- Accepting limitations on your control
- Moderating expectations
- Not caring about outcomes

Instead it's about:
- Active self-observation
- Building internal validation
- Creating positive feedback loops through behavior change

### On Outsourcing Validation

> "Once you start actively looking for things to appreciate about yourself, you realize how you've outsourced that task to other people."

The insight: Others are never paying as much attention to you as you are. "There's a lot about you to appreciate that goes unremarked upon if you wait for someone else to acknowledge it."

This reframes external validation not as bad, but as insufficient—supplemented by becoming "your biggest fan."

### Connection to Spiral Psychology

This practice directly addresses the "calcification" problem from the spirals framework:

- **Spirals**: Negative construal → negative behavior → negative feedback → entrenched beliefs
- **Appreciation practice**: Positive construal → positive behavior → internal positive feedback → reinforced positive self-image

The practice creates an upward spiral by:
1. Actively seeking evidence of positive self-worth (combats confirmation bias toward negative)
2. Changing behavior in anticipation of evening reflection (breaks calcification cycle)
3. Building a "log" for bad days (evidence against spiraling down)

### Key Insight on Practice vs. Perfection

> "I've wandered away from many practices only to return to them, usually because I miss them, because seeing the benefits of doing something is often not as powerful as experiencing its absence."

This is more psychologically honest than the typical self-help framing of "build habits through discipline." The author acknowledges imperfection—"I've done the first day of 'Yoga With Adriene' 30-day challenge at least 30 times"—while still finding value in returning.

### Practical Notes

- Not every day needs rich content—appreciating small things counts
- Initial resistance is normal ("sounds self-involved, maybe pathetic")
- The cumulative effect matters more than any single entry
- Works best as a "thing you do" rather than a rigid practice

## Grievance Culture and the Victim-Righteousness Fallacy (Pamela Paul, NYT)

*Source: https://www.nytimes.com/2024/04/25/opinion/oppression-victim-offensive.html - Added: 2026-01-18*

A critique of grievance culture—how feeling wronged has become confused with being right.

### The Golden Age of Aggrievement

> "No matter who you are or what your politics, whatever your ethnic origin, economic circumstance, family history or mental health status, chances are you have ample reason to be ticked off."

The framing differs by political orientation:
- **Left**: oppressed, marginalized, silenced, erased, harmed, traumatized
- **Right**: ignored, overlooked, demeaned, maligned; in Trumpspeak: wronged and betrayed

But both sides share the underlying dynamic: leveraging powerlessness as a form of power.

### The Oppression Olympics

Building on Frank Bruni's concept in *The Age of Grievance*:

> "The idea that people occupying different rungs of privilege or victimization can't possibly grasp life elsewhere on the ladder."

This manifests in practices like "progressive stacking" (inverting assumed privilege hierarchies). The problematic questions it raises:
- Who determines the hierarchy?
- Who is more oppressed—an older, disabled white veteran or a young, gay Latino man?
- What does it mean that winning requires proving how hard off you are?

### The Simplistic Binary

> "Individuals as well as tribes, ethnic groups and nations are divvied up into simplistic binaries: colonizer vs. colonized, oppressor vs. oppressed, privileged and not."

People can "determine, perform and weaponize their grievance" knowing they'll be rewarded with attention—if not substantive improvement.

### The Core Fallacy

As Ricky Gervais put it: **"Just because you're offended, doesn't mean you're right."**

The article's central insight:
- Being oppressed doesn't necessarily make you good
- "Might is right" is wrong, but so is "victim is right"
- Having been victimized doesn't give you a pass

### How Grievance Undermines Progress

From a Harvard Political Review undergraduate:
> "In pitting subjugated groups against one another, the Oppression Olympics not only reduce the store of resources to which groups and movements have access, but also breed intersectional bitterness that facilitates further injustice."

Concrete examples of grievance obscuring rather than solving problems:
- **Banko Brown case (SF)**: A tragedy involving a Black, homeless, transgender man became a referendum on everything at once—anti-trans, anti-homeless, racist—rather than addressing any specific issue
- **Brooklyn dog-killing**: Concerns about public safety were read as "racist vigilantism," doing nothing to address homelessness, safety, or mental health

### The Feedback Loop

Goading people into constant alarmism:
1. Distracts from actual wrongdoing
2. Turns complex tragedies into box-checking contests
3. Leaves people endlessly stewing
4. Creates more outrage, satisfying no one
5. "Even those who hate Tucker Carlson become Tucker Carlson"

### Connection to Self-Help Critique

This connects to the broader themes in this file:

**Parallels to pop-Stoicism critique:**
- Both grievance culture and self-help can be forms of self-focus that distract from collective action
- Both can be "self-indulgent"—one through excessive victimhood, the other through excessive acceptance
- Both fail to address structural issues while feeling like they're doing something

**The underlying tension:**
- Recognizing real injustice vs. performing grievance
- Individual pain vs. collective progress
- Validation vs. action

### Key Takeaway

> "If we continue to mistake grievance for righteousness, we only set ourselves up for more of the same."

The critique isn't that grievances are invalid—"plenty of the dissatisfaction is justified"—but that grievance alone doesn't constitute a moral position or a path forward.

## "Sorting the Self" - The Psychometrics Critique (Christopher Yates, Hedgehog Review)

*Source: https://hedgehogreview.com/issues/missing-character/articles/sorting-the-self - Added: 2026-01-20*

A scholarly critique of the personality assessment industry and our cultural devotion to self-classification through typological tools. Yates traces how the self became an object of codification and asks what we lose by reducing identity to acronyms.

### The Genealogy of Personality Assessment

Three paradigms converged to create our current "personalityism":

**1. The Aptitudinal Paradigm**
- Ancient Chinese civil service exams → British/US civil service testing
- WWI: Woodworth Personal Data Sheet for screening soldiers
- Focus: What can you *do*?
- Gradually shifted from "character" (duty, virtue) to "personality" (traits, tendencies)

**2. The Psychological Paradigm**
- 1879: Wundt's laboratory focused on quantifying personhood
- Jung's *Psychological Types* (1921): extroversion/introversion, thinking/feeling
- Cattell's multivariate trait analysis: 16 factor model
- Evolution: MBTI → Big Five → modern inventories

**3. The Commercial Paradigm**
- 1915-17: Industrial psychology emerges
- Münsterberg, Scott: personality as workforce optimization
- 1950s: Manufacturing embraces "human engineering"
- Today: 80% of Fortune 500 use psychometric assessments; $6.5B industry by 2027

### The Epistemic Leap of Faith

> "What start out as qualifying statements become parenthetical caveats, which then shrink to ellipses, which in turn become periods, then acronyms."

The slippage: from conditional ("It's as though you are...") to declarative ("You are an...").

The question Yates raises: If A represents "outgoing" and profiling says "outgoing" corresponds to you, does "You are an A" carry the authority of an equal sign? Does an assemblage of behavioral properties represent one's essence?

> "Would we presume, by the same token, to define the earth's existence on the basis of its weather patterns?"

### William H. Whyte's Early Critique (1956)

In *The Organization Man*, Whyte saw the whole enterprise as:
- Jargony, disingenuous, reductive
- A cover for identifying "loyalty" markers
- A "positivistic myth" supporting power imbalances

The object was no longer to identify what you could *do* but who you *are*—and crucially, what role you might *perform* (note the etymology of "personality" from *persona*: the mask worn by actors).

### The "Abbreviational Paradigm"

> "At what ongoing price do we abbreviate the self?"

The person becomes a personality, and personality—"in principle, a conditional abstraction"—becomes an ascertainable essence. The self is flattened into a brand without mystery.

> "We mistake impressions for absolutes."

### Why We Submit: Rieff and Taylor

**Philip Rieff (1966)**: After WWII, loss of shared normative frameworks created "psychological man"—seeking individual satisfaction and self-realization rather than duty to social order. Personality instruments salved this existential wound by codifying the individual, shortcutting the long haul of internal reconfiguration.

**Charles Taylor**: We've become "buffered subjects" in a disenchanted, mechanistic world, anchored in "disciplined self-remaking." But this creates malaise—"a wide sense that something may have been lost."

### The Paradox

> "How makeshift it is, yet so natural, that we now search for ourselves in the axioms of powerful 'instruments' and the 'profiles' they patch together."

We take the Good Life to be one in which we assert and instrumentalize our individuality. But treating our inner lives on the basis of scorable traits leaves us *more* detached from those inner lives, not less.

### Connection to Self-Help Critique

The personality assessment industry shares DNA with pop-Stoicism and self-help philosophy:

**Same underlying need**: A response to loss of shared frameworks, seeking secular answers to "Who am I?"

**Same reductive move**: Complex human experience → actionable framework → acronym/number

**Same epistemic sleight**: Conditional speculation presented as declarative certainty

**Same commercial success**: The "lure of the miraculous" is lucrative

### The Alternative

Yates doesn't reject therapeutic work or self-understanding. The critique is of:
1. The leap from speculation to certainty
2. The reduction of substance to properties
3. The abbreviation of mystery

What's needed is "a long and probing engagement" with the difficult condition of being a self—not shortcuts that feel like recognition but flatten rather than illuminate.

### Key Quotes

> "We are unknown to ourselves, we knowers…and there is good reason for this. We have never looked for ourselves—so how are we ever supposed to find ourselves?" —Nietzsche

> "The whole human enterprise seems indeed to consist in man's proving to himself every moment that he is a man and not a sprig!" —Dostoevsky

> "How we crave for a noticing glance!" —Goethe

> "The copula 'is' does not carry the authority of an equal sign."

### About the Author

Christopher Yates is a research fellow at the Institute for Advanced Studies in Culture and teaches philosophy at James Madison University. His research focuses on imagination and longing in human reason and belief.

## "What Does Your Philosophy Maximize?" (LessWrong)

*Source: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/qeRvKss7nwhxrR4mk/what-does-your-philosophy-maximize - Added: 2026-01-20*

A reframing of philosophical disagreement: schools of thought as maximization algorithms for different values. Instead of seeing opposing philosophies as misguided, recognize that people are optimizing for different metrics entirely.

### The Core Insight

> "I see philosophical schools as maximization algorithms. You subscribe to a philosophy when its algorithm maximizes what you desire most."

Common philosophical schools and their optimization targets:
- **Empiricism/Rationalism**: Maximize predictive power
- **Theism**: Maximize mental integrity/internal peace
- **Atheism**: Maximize freedom of thought

### Why Philosophical Conflict Feels Visceral

When someone's metric *correlates positively* with yours, you can tolerate them. When their metric is *negatively correlated* with yours, you feel disdain—even a desire to correct their lack of "common sense."

> "The reason you see others outside your school of thought as misguided fools is because they are maximizing an entirely different metric to the one you are, perhaps unwittingly, maximizing yourself."

This explains:
- Theists seeing non-believers as "lacking love of God"
- Atheists seeing believers as "sheep following old scriptures"
- Empiricists seeing others as "blinded by ideals"

### The Alternative Approach

Rather than trying to convert people:

> "Before trying to convert someone to my school of thought, first I ask myself: does this person necessarily want to maximize what I am maximizing? Or would they be better off maximizing whatever they are doing right now?"

The key recognition:
- No universal rule says predictive power is more useful than internal peace
- No reason to convince a happy friend to become a rationalist
- Conversion attempts often waste energy

### Connection to Other Ideas

- **Complements "What is your personal totalizing worldview?"**: That piece asks you to articulate *your* philosophy; this piece asks why others have *different* philosophies
- **Extends Baker's critique**: Baker argues self-help is ideology masking structural conditions; this piece goes further—even "correct" philosophies may be optimizing the wrong metric for certain people
- **Relates to "You Are Not Too Irrational"**: Both argue against weaponizing your framework against others—that piece defends preferences, this defends philosophical divergence
- **Echoes the psychoanalysis entry**: Psychoanalysis insists on the particularity of each case; this piece makes a similar move at the philosophical level—each person's optimization target is particular
- **Tension with Rawlsian liberalism**: Lefebvre argues liberalism should be recognized as "your" worldview if unchurched; this piece would ask: what if internal peace (theism) maximizes what some people actually want?

### Practical Takeaways

1. **Diagnose before prescribing** - Figure out what someone is optimizing for before recommending your philosophy
2. **Check for correlation** - Your disdain for someone's beliefs may indicate negatively correlated metrics, not their error
3. **Accept different optima** - A happy life following a "wrong" philosophy is still a happy life
4. **Self-reflect on your own target** - What are *you* actually maximizing? Is it what you claim to value?
